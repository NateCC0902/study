1The plant: the boat and its model

① Intuition what you're steering

The "plant" is the thing being controlled. For us it's a boat that turns by rudder, and the central, course-long fact is this: the rudder sets a turning rate, not a heading. Everything downstream is a consequence.

$$ T\,\dot r + r = K\,(\delta + d), \qquad \dot\psi = r $$

That's the Nomoto model: a single first-order lag from rudder to yaw rate, then an integral from yaw rate to heading. Set $\dot r = 0$ and the steady turning rate is $r_\infty = K(\delta + d)$ — the boat turns forever unless net rudder is zero.

TermMeaning
PlantThe system being controlled. Here, the USV's steering dynamics — rudder in, heading out. The controller's job is to make the plant do what you want despite its lag and disturbances.
USVUnmanned surface vessel — the autonomous boat we're holding on a heading. Stands in for anything that steers by turning rate: quadcopter yaw, car heading, a tank turret.
Nomoto modelThe standard first-order ship-steering model, $T\dot r + r = K\delta$. The simplest equation that still behaves like a real boat: yaw rate lags the rudder through one time constant.
$K$ — turning gainSteady yaw rate per unit rudder, units $\frac{\text{rad/s}}{\text{rad}}$. Our boat: $K=0.6$, so a 10° rudder yields a $\sim 6$ °/s steady turn.
$T$ — time constantHow sluggishly the hull responds, in seconds. Our boat: $T=2.5$ s. After one $T$ the yaw rate has reached $\sim 63\%$ of its final value; after $3T$, $\sim 95\%$.
Rudder angle $\delta$The control input you actually command (rad or degrees). Not the heading — the rudder commands turning.
Yaw rate $r$How fast the boat is turning, $\dot\psi$, in rad/s (or °/s). The rudder controls this directly through $K$ and $T$.
Heading $\psi$Which way the bow points, in rad (or degrees). The integral of yaw rate, $\psi=\int r\,dt$ — which is why a fixed rudder makes heading ramp forever.
Disturbance $d$An external push — current or wind — modeled as a constant offset on the rudder, $\equiv 4°$ stuck rudder on our boat. It acts exactly like a bias the controller must cancel.
flowchart LR D["rudder δ"] -->|"gain K, lag T"| R["yaw rate r"] R -->|"∫ dt"| P["heading ψ"] W["current / wind d"] --> R
💡 The one fact behind every other term

Rudder → rate, rate → (integrated) heading. The boat carries momentum into wherever you point it, and it responds late. Lag + accumulation = overshoot. Most of this glossary is vocabulary for fighting that single sentence.

2The three terms: P, I, D and what they read as

② Math the law and its units

The discrete control law we run every $dt$ on a microcontroller, with error $e = \psi_\text{set} - \psi$:

$$ u_n = k_p\,e_n + k_i \underbrace{\textstyle\sum_k e_k\,dt}_{\text{integral}} + k_d \underbrace{\frac{e_n - e_{n-1}}{dt}}_{\text{derivative}}, \qquad u \leftarrow \mathrm{clamp}(u,\,-\delta_\max,\,+\delta_\max) $$

Each gain has units, and reading them physically makes tuning far less mystical:

TermMeaning
Setpoint $\psi_\text{set}$The heading you want the boat to hold. The reference the controller chases.
Error $e$$e = \psi_\text{set} - \psi$, in rad. Always angle-wrapped before it enters the PID (see §3). Zero error is the goal; the sign tells you which way to turn.
$k_p$ — proportional gainUnits $\frac{\text{rad rudder}}{\text{rad error}}$ (dimensionless). Reads as "how much rudder per degree I'm off." More $k_p$ = faster response, less offset, more ringing — until it never stops ringing.
$k_i$ — integral gainUnits $\frac{\text{rad rudder}}{\text{rad}\cdot\text{s}}$ (i.e. $1/\text{s}$). "How hard I lean on a persistent error." Should be the slowest thing in the loop — it's a memory, and big $k_i$ means jumpy conclusions from short memory.
$k_d$ — derivative gainUnits $\frac{\text{rad rudder}}{\text{rad/s}}$ (i.e. seconds). "How much rudder per degree-per-second of swing." Acts only on change; once on heading and steady, $D=0$.
P term ($k_p e$)The spring. Force proportional to how far off you are. Strong, immediate, but at zero error it outputs zero rudder — so it can't hold against a disturbance.
I term ($k_i \textstyle\sum e\,dt$)The trim tab. Accumulates lingering error and ramps the rudder to whatever steady value cancels the disturbance. On our boat it learns the $-4°$ trim needed for the current, then holds it at zero error.
D term ($k_d \,\dot e$)The damper. Opposes fast change, so it brakes the approach and kills overshoot. Negative when error is shrinking quickly — that's the cancellation that is damping.
control step (per dt) pseudocode
error      = wrap(setpoint - heading)        # rad, ALWAYS wrapped (see §3)
integral  += error * dt                       # rad·s, persists across calls
derivative = (error - prev_error) / dt        # rad/s

u = kp*error + ki*integral + kd*derivative    # rad of rudder
u = clamp(u, -RUDDER_MAX, +RUDDER_MAX)
prev_error = error
∑ P and D cancel during a good approach

Mid-turn (heading 60°, setpoint 90°, $k_p=2$, $k_d=3$): $P=+1.047$ rad pushes in, but the bow is already closing at $\sim 20$ °/s, so $D=-1.047$ rad pulls back. They exactly cancel and the controller nearly coasts — pushing harder would only overshoot harder. That cancellation is the whole point of D.

3Failure modes & their fixes

④ Real case the bugs everyone meets

These are the named ways a naive PID misbehaves on a real boat — and the standard fix for each. Every one of them is a consequence of §1's two facts colliding with real actuators and sensors.

TermMeaning
Steady-state offsetThe leftover error a P-only controller settles at. To hold heading against the current the rudder must hold $-4°$, but $u=k_p e$ can only do that by keeping $e_\text{ss} = -4°/k_p \ne 0$. P holds course by staying wrong. Fixed by I.
OvershootSailing past the setpoint before settling. Caused by momentum into the target plus lag. Tamed by D (and by not over-driving P).
Settle timeHow long until the boat stays within a small band of the setpoint. The thing you trade against overshoot and chatter when tuning.
Integral windupWhile the rudder is saturated, the I term keeps accumulating error it can't act on. On a 0°→180° turn the rudder pins at $+35°$ for $\sim 9$ s; the integral can store $\sim 13$ rad $\approx 750°$ of commanded rudder. The boat then does donuts forever. Fixed by anti-windup.
Anti-windupAny scheme that stops the integral from bloating during saturation. Three standard forms: clamp (cap the integral so $k_i\cdot\text{integral}$ can't exceed the rudder limit), conditional integration (don't integrate on saturated samples), back-calculation (subtract $(u_\text{unclamped}-u_\text{clamped})\cdot k$ from the integral each step — industry standard, smoothest).
SaturationThe actuator hitting its physical limit, $\pm 35°$ rudder. The command can ask for more; the rudder simply can't deliver it. The root cause of windup.
Derivative kickWhen the setpoint steps, the error jumps in one sample, so $\dot e$ spikes. A 10° setpoint step gives $10°/0.05\,\text{s} = 200$ °/s of fake derivative $\approx 600°$ of rudder — a useless spike that slams the servo. The boat didn't swing; your setpoint moved.
Derivative-on-measurementThe fix for kick: differentiate $\psi$ instead of $e$. When the setpoint is constant $\dot e = -\dot\psi$, so it's the same damping signal — but it ignores setpoint jumps. One sign flip: d = -(meas - prev_meas)/dt.
Low-pass filterSmooths the derivative against sensor noise. The derivative of noise is huge ($\sigma=0.5°$ noise → $\sim 14$ °/s of garbage $\to \times k_d \approx 42°$ of rudder chatter). The filter $d_f \leftarrow \alpha\,d_f + (1-\alpha)\,d_\text{raw}$ ($\alpha\approx0.85$) cuts it, at the cost of a little lag in D.
Angle wrappingMapping an angle error into $(-\pi,\pi]$ so the boat turns the short way. Heading 350°, setpoint 10° is 20° away, but naive subtraction gives $-340°$ — "turn the long way," a crash near a dock. wrap(a) = (a+π) % (2π) − π, applied to every error.
the three fixes, in code Python
import math

def wrap(angle):                                  # → (-pi, pi], the short way
    return (angle + math.pi) % (2 * math.pi) - math.pi

# derivative-on-measurement (kills derivative kick on setpoint steps)
derivative = -(measurement - prev_measurement) / dt

# low-pass the derivative (kills noise amplification)
d_filtered = alpha * d_filtered + (1 - alpha) * d_raw   # alpha ≈ 0.85

# anti-windup, clamp form: cap the integral so ki*integral
# can never command more than the rudder can deliver
integral = max(-RUDDER_MAX / ki, min(RUDDER_MAX / ki, integral))
⚠️ The two that are invisible until they aren't

Angle wrapping and a stale integral both pass every test that doesn't happen to trigger them — then crash the boat the first time it crosses north or is re-engaged after sitting. Wrap every error; reset() the controller on re-engage.

4Tuning: gains, limits, and the recipe

⑤ Practice how to pick gains

Tuning is the art of choosing $k_p, k_i, k_d$ for a fast, smooth, offset-free response. The vocabulary below is what the recipe is phrased in.

TermMeaning
Ultimate gain $K_u$The $k_p$ at which the closed loop oscillates with constant amplitude (P-only, $k_i=k_d=0$). On our boat $K_u \approx 5$. The reference point for Ziegler–Nichols.
Ultimate period $T_u$The period of that sustained oscillation at $K_u$. On our boat $T_u \approx 9$ s. Feeds the ZN formulas.
Ziegler–NicholsA classic tuning rule that computes gains from $K_u$ and $T_u$: $k_p=0.6K_u$, $k_i=1.2K_u/T_u$, $k_d=0.075K_uT_u$. From our $K_u=5,\,T_u=9$ that's $k_p=3.0,\,k_i=0.67,\,k_d=3.4$. It targets fast disturbance rejection and tolerates overshoot — a hot starting point to de-tune from.
Phase lagThe delay between command and effect: servo slew, sensor-filter delay, comms latency, hull dynamics. Phase lag at high gain is the universal recipe for oscillation. More lag → lower usable $k_p$. It's why "raise $k_p$ until it oscillates" works even when the ideal Nomoto plant alone never would.
Slew rateThe fastest the actuator can move, $25$ °/s for our servo — so $\sim 2.8$ s stop-to-stop. At high gain the command flips faster than the rudder can follow, turning a square-wave command into a lagging sawtooth: phase lag in the flesh.
🛠️ The recipe, in order

(1) $k_i=0,\,k_d=0$; raise $k_p$ to sustained oscillation ($\approx K_u=5$), then halve to $k_p \approx 2\text{–}2.5$. (2) Raise $k_d$ until overshoot is gone ($\approx 3$). (3) Add the smallest $k_i$ that removes offset in reasonable time ($0.2$, trims the current in $\sim 30$ s). Result: $k_p=2,\ k_i=0.2,\ k_d=3$. Field order is identical: P → D → I, starting low.

5Deployment: from sim to a real USV

④ Real case on the water

The math doesn't change going to hardware; the plumbing does. These are the terms you meet when the controller leaves the simulator and runs in a ROS 2 node.

In the simOn the boat (ROS 2)
psi (heading)Yaw from the IMU / /odometry/filtered — already fused upstream by an EKF or complementary filter.
wrap(sp - psi)Same wrap, done in your node every cycle, on every error.
DT = 0.05Timer callback at $\sim 20\text{–}50$ Hz — but use the measured $dt$, not the nominal one.
rudder $\delta$A rudder servo, or differential thrust: left = base + u, right = base - u.
RUDDER_MAXYour real actuator limits — clamp in software too, and pair with anti-windup.
current disturbance $d$Real current and wind — the I term's standing job to trim out.
🔑 Two non-negotiables before any autonomy test

Have a hardware kill switch wired and tested. And reset() the PID whenever the controller is re-engaged — a stale integral from five minutes ago is a windup bug waiting to fling the boat at the dock.

6Quick check

Q1 Match the symptom to the cure
A PD-tuned boat holds 92° instead of the commanded 90° in a steady current, and never closes the last 2°. Which glossary term names the problem, and which fixes it?
  • Derivative kick; fixed by derivative-on-measurement.
  • Steady-state offset; fixed by adding the integral (I) term.
  • Integral windup; fixed by clamping the integral.
  • Angle wrapping; fixed by wrapping the error.
A P (or PD) controller outputs $k_p e$, so to hold the $-4°$ of rudder that cancels the current it must keep a non-zero error: $e_\text{ss} = -4°/k_p$. That's steady-state offset. D only acts on change and dies at steady state, so it can't help. The integral ramps the rudder to the trim value and lets error return to zero — exactly what I is for. (Windup and kick are I-side and setpoint-step problems; wrapping is a turn-the-short-way problem — none match this symptom.)
