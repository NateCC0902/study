The Plant & Open Loop: the boat you're about to tame

Before a single gain, you need a correct mental model of what you're steering. We build the boat from its physics, watch it drift with no controller, then write down the exact control law you'll spend the rest of the course tuning.

1The boat: one model, used in every case

① Intuition build the picture first

Our "plant" — control-speak for the thing being controlled — is an unmanned surface vessel (USV) holding a compass heading. You don't command heading directly; you command a rudder angle $\delta$. The rudder makes the hull turn at some yaw rate $r$, and heading $\psi$ is whatever that turning accumulates to. That one sentence is the source of every difficulty to come.

💡 The one-sentence model

The rudder sets a turning rate, not a heading. Heading is the running total of turning — so the boat always has momentum heading into wherever you point it.

We use the simplest model that still behaves like a real boat: the first-order Nomoto model. It says the yaw rate lags the rudder through a single time constant, and heading is the integral of yaw rate.

flowchart LR D["rudder δ"] -->|"gain K, lag T"| R["yaw rate r"] R -->|"∫ dt"| P["heading ψ"] W["current / wind"] --> R
② Math derive it

The Nomoto equation and the heading integral are just two coupled first-order ODEs:

$$ T\,\dot r + r = K\,(\delta + d), \qquad \dot\psi = r $$

where $K$ is the steady turning gain (yaw rate per unit rudder), $T$ is the time constant (how sluggishly the hull responds), and $d$ is a constant disturbance — current or wind — that acts just like a stuck offset on the rudder. Read the steady state: set $\dot r = 0$ and the boat turns at $r_\infty = K(\delta+d)$ forever. There is no heading that makes $\dot\psi = 0$ unless $r = 0$, i.e. unless total rudder-plus-disturbance is zero.

2Open loop: what the boat does on its own

① Intuition hands off the wheel

Hold the rudder at a fixed angle, take your hands off, and watch. There's no controller here — just the plant. Drag the slider and read the two panels: heading on top, rudder on the bottom.

🧭 Open-loop heading (fixed rudder, no controller)
🔑 What to notice

Yaw rate doesn't jump to its final value — it climbs there, reaching ~63% at $t=T=2.5$ s (that's what a first-order lag does). And heading never settles: it ramps forever, because heading is the integral of a non-zero turning rate. Set the rudder to 0° and it finally stops — but then you can't steer anywhere.

④ Real case why this matters

This is every vehicle that steers by rate: boats, quadcopter yaw, car heading, a tank turret. You can never "set heading" open-loop; you'd have to time the rudder perfectly and undo it perfectly. The instant there's a disturbance, you're off. That's why we close the loop.

3The two facts that cause everything

Keep these in your head — every later chapter is a fight against them:

  1. The rudder controls rate, not heading. You command turning; heading accumulates. So the boat carries momentum into the target.
  2. The response lags. Whatever the rudder does now, the hull keeps doing the old thing for a couple of seconds ($T=2.5$ s).
⚠️ Lag + accumulation = overshoot

Momentum into the setpoint plus a delayed response is the universal recipe for overshoot and ringing. A good controller doesn't remove these facts — it anticipates them.

4The control law you'll implement

② Math the discrete PID

Continuous PID is $u(t) = k_p e + k_i\!\int e\,dt + k_d \dfrac{de}{dt}$, with error $e = \psi_\text{set} - \psi$. We run it on a microcontroller every $dt$, so we discretize: the integral becomes a running sum, the derivative a finite difference.

$$ u_n = k_p e_n + k_i \underbrace{\textstyle\sum_k e_k\,dt}_{\text{integral}} + k_d \underbrace{\frac{e_n - e_{n-1}}{dt}}_{\text{derivative}}, \qquad u \leftarrow \mathrm{clamp}(u,\,-\delta_\max,\,+\delta_\max) $$
control step (per dt) pseudocode
error      = wrap(setpoint - heading)        # rad, ALWAYS wrapped (Ch 07)
integral  += error * dt                       # rad·s, persists across calls
derivative = (error - prev_error) / dt        # rad/s

u = kp*error + ki*integral + kd*derivative    # rad of rudder
u = clamp(u, -RUDDER_MAX, +RUDDER_MAX)
prev_error = error

The three gains have units, and reading them physically makes tuning far less mystical:

GainUnitsReads as
$k_p$rad rudder / rad error"how much rudder per degree I'm off"
$k_i$rad rudder / (rad·s)"how hard I lean on a persistent error"
$k_d$rad rudder / (rad/s)"how much rudder per degree-per-second of swing"

∑ Read the result again

The boat is still 30° from the setpoint, yet P and D exactly cancel and the controller nearly coasts. The bow is already swinging in at 20 °/s — pushing harder would just overshoot harder. That cancellation is damping. P alone would have commanded 60°, clamped to 35°, full speed into the overshoot.

6The PID, in code

③ Code read every line

Here is the controller as a small, reusable class — the same shape you'd drop into a ROS 2 node. The state (integral, prev_error) is what makes it a controller and not a formula: it remembers.

pid_controller.py Python
class PID:
    def __init__(self, kp, ki, kd, output_limits=(-1.0, 1.0)):
        self.kp, self.ki, self.kd = kp, ki, kd
        self.out_min, self.out_max = output_limits
        self.integral = 0.0
        self.prev_error = None          # None = no previous sample yet

    def reset(self):
        """Call when the controller is re-engaged after being off."""
        self.integral = 0.0
        self.prev_error = None

    def update(self, error, dt):
        """One control step. error is already angle-wrapped (rad)."""
        p = self.kp * error
        self.integral += error * dt
        i = self.ki * self.integral
        d = 0.0 if self.prev_error is None else self.kd * (error - self.prev_error) / dt
        self.prev_error = error
        u = p + i + d
        return max(self.out_min, min(self.out_max, u))   # clamp
🛠️ Two design choices live inside update()

This version is the honest skeleton. Anti-windup (Ch 05) and derivative-on-measurement (Ch 06) are decisions you'll add here. We'll meet the bugs that motivate each one before we fix them.

7Your turn

Q1 Conceptual check
In the open-loop sim, why does heading ramp forever instead of settling at some angle?
  • The model is unstable — any rudder makes it blow up.
  • Heading is the integral of a non-zero yaw rate, so it accumulates without bound.
  • The disturbance keeps growing over time.
  • The rudder slew limit prevents it from stopping.
A fixed rudder gives a fixed steady yaw rate $r_\infty = K\delta$. Heading $\psi = \int r\,dt$ then climbs linearly forever. Only $r = 0$ (zero net rudder) stops it — which is why you need feedback to hold a heading.
Q2 Compute it
With $K = 0.6$ (rad/s)/rad and a steady rudder of $10° = 0.1745$ rad, what is the steady-state yaw rate in degrees per second? (Hint: $r_\infty = K\delta$, then convert.)
°/s
$r_\infty = 0.6 \times 0.1745 = 0.1047$ rad/s $= 6.0$ °/s. The hull reaches ~63% of this (3.8 °/s) after $T = 2.5$ s and ~95% after $3T$.
Q3 Derivation — discretize the integral term
The continuous I term is $k_i\int_0^t e(\tau)\,d\tau$. Show how it becomes the running sum integral += error*dt in code, and say what variable must persist between calls.
Hint
A definite integral over $[0,t]$ is the area under $e(\tau)$. Approximate that area with rectangles of width $dt$ and height $e_k$.
Show one solution

Rectangle (left-Riemann) approximation: $\int_0^{t_n} e\,d\tau \approx \sum_{k=0}^{n} e_k\,dt$. Each step adds one rectangle $e_n\,dt$ to the previous total, which is exactly integral += error*dt. The accumulator self.integral must persist across update() calls — it is the controller's memory of how long the error has lingered. Reset it (reset()) whenever the controller is re-engaged, or a stale sum from minutes ago becomes a windup bug.

🔑 Chapter recap

The boat is a first-order Nomoto plant: rudder → (lagged) yaw rate → (integrated) heading, plus a constant current. Open-loop it ramps forever — you must close the loop. The discrete PID law sums P, I, D and clamps to the rudder limits; its state (integral, prev_error) is its memory. Next: turn on just the P term and meet the spring.
