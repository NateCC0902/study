Tuning & Deploying to a Real USV (ROS 2)

1The recipe: P, then D, then I — in that order

① Intuition why this order, not any other

Tuning is not a search over a 3-D gain space; that way lies madness. It's a sequence, and the sequence falls straight out of what each term does. P sets the aggression. D tames the overshoot P creates. I cleans up the steady offset that's left over once P and D have settled the transient. Touch them out of order and you'll be chasing your own tail — D has nothing to damp until P is doing the turning, and I will lie to you about offset while the transient is still ringing.

💡 One sentence per term

P does the turn. D stops the turn from overshooting. I erases the leftover bias that neither P nor D can. Tune them in the order they take effect: fast (P), faster (D), slowest (I).

④ Real case the gains we landed on

Every number below comes from this boat — the first-order Nomoto plant with $K=0.6$, $T=2.5$ s, a ±35° rudder slewing at 25 °/s, and a current that pushes the bow like 4° of stuck rudder. Run the recipe and you converge on the gains used all through Cases 3–7: $k_p=2$, $k_i=0.2$, $k_d=3$. The rest of this section is how you get there, step by step, so you can repeat it on any boat.

2Step 1 — Raise $k_p$ to the ultimate gain, then halve

① Intuition find the edge, back off

Set $k_i=0$ and $k_d=0$ — pure P. Now raise $k_p$ until the boat does sustained, constant-amplitude oscillation: not decaying, not growing, just ringing forever. That gain is the ultimate gain $K_u$, and the period of the ringing is the ultimate period $T_u$. On this boat that's $K_u \approx 5$ with $T_u \approx 9$ s. (Recall from Case 1: $k_p=2$ rings once and settles; $k_p=5$ never settles, oscillating ±16° forever; $k_p=8$ rings harder and faster, ±25°.)

🔑 Why an ideal plant still oscillates

A bare Nomoto boat with a perfect rudder never goes unstable from $k_p$ alone — even $k_p=100$ would settle. Real boats oscillate because the actuator lags: our servo slews at 25 °/s, so at high $k_p$ the command flips rail-to-rail faster than the rudder can follow. That phase lag at high gain is the universal recipe for oscillation (Case 9). The slew limit is baked into the sim for exactly this reason.

② Math halve to get a usable gain

The ultimate gain is the edge of stability — useless on its own. Back off to half:

$$ k_p = \tfrac{1}{2}\,K_u = \tfrac{1}{2}\times 5 = 2.5 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \text{settle on } k_p \in [2,\,2.5] $$

Halving the ultimate gain is the oldest trick in the book — it's exactly what Ziegler–Nichols does for a pure-P controller ($k_p = 0.5\,K_u$). It buys you a comfortable stability margin: brisk response, one small overshoot, then it settles. We'll nudge it to $k_p=2$ for a touch more margin since D and I are still coming.

⚠️ Don't try to crank $k_p$ to kill offset

You will be tempted: P leaves a steady offset (Case 2), and bigger $k_p$ shrinks it ($e_{ss} = -4°/k_p$). But $k_p \ge 5$ oscillates — so with any usable $k_p$ the offset is structural. Don't fight it with P. That's I's job, two steps from now.

3Step 2 — Raise $k_d$ until the overshoot dies

① Intuition add the damper

Hold $k_p=2$. Now add D and sweep it up. D acts only on change: when the error is shrinking fast, the derivative is negative, so D opposes P during the approach and the boat eases off before it arrives. From Case 3:

  • $k_d=0$ — overshoots to ~118°, rings.
  • $k_d=1$ — one small overshoot.
  • $k_d=3$ — glides in like a parking valet. No overshoot.

Stop when the overshoot is gone. Push $k_d$ past that and you buy nothing but twitchiness and noise sensitivity (Case 7) — D differentiates compass noise into rudder garbage.

② Math the coasting condition

The controller goes neutral — coasts — when the P push and the D brake exactly cancel:

$$ k_p\,e = k_d\,\dot e \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \dot e = \frac{k_p}{k_d}\,e = \frac{2}{3}\,e $$

So at $k_p=2,\ k_d=3$ the controller stops pushing once the closing rate (°/s) exceeds ⅔ of the remaining error (°). 30° out, closing faster than 20 °/s → it's already easing off. That cancellation is damping — re-read the by-hand timestep in Ch 01 where P and D exactly cancel 60° apiece while the boat is still 30° from target.

🛠️ D can't do everything

D only tames motion. Once you're on heading and steady, $\dot e = 0$ and D contributes nothing. It cannot do the turn (that's P) and it cannot fix the steady offset (that's I). If you find yourself raising $k_d$ to fix a constant bias, stop — you're using the wrong term.

4Step 3 — Add the smallest $k_i$ that erases the offset

① Intuition the trim tab

PD holds course by staying wrong: the P term can only output the −4° of rudder needed to cancel the current by keeping the error at −2°. I fixes this. The integral accumulates the persistent error and ramps the rudder to −4° on its own — it learns the current. With I carrying the trim, the error returns to zero and P relaxes to zero. On this boat $k_i=0.2$ trims the current out in ~30 s.

🔑 Smallest that works — and why

The integral is a memory, and it should be the slowest thing in the loop. Big $k_i$ = jumpy conclusions from a short memory = overshoot and slow weaving (try $k_i=2$ in the sim and watch it wander). Add only as much I as you need to kill the offset in a reasonable time. If you see slow weaving, halve $k_i$. That ~30 s settling isn't a bug — you want I to respond to things that persist (current, wind, trim), not to every wave.

② Math the offset I is paid to remove

To hold a steady heading the net yaw moment must be zero, so the rudder must hold −4° to cancel the current. A pure-P controller delivers that only through error:

$$ k_p\,e_{ss} = -4° \quad\Longrightarrow\quad e_{ss} = -\frac{4°}{k_p} $$

At $k_p=2$ that's a stubborn −2° offset that P and D will never remove. The integral term, $k_i\sum e\,dt$, keeps growing as long as any error remains — so it can only stop growing when $e=0$. That's the whole trick: I is the only term that holds nonzero rudder at zero error.

5Ziegler–Nichols: the same $K_u, T_u$, by formula

② Math plug the ultimate values into the table

You already measured everything Ziegler–Nichols needs in Step 1: $K_u=5$ and $T_u=9$ s. The classic ZN tuning rules turn those two numbers directly into gains:

$$ k_p = 0.6\,K_u, \qquad k_i = \frac{1.2\,K_u}{T_u}, \qquad k_d = 0.075\,K_u\,T_u $$

Run the arithmetic with $K_u=5,\ T_u=9$:

$k_d$ agrees almost exactly (3.4 vs 3.0) — both methods see the same damping need. But ZN's $k_p$ and especially its $k_i$ run hot: $k_i=0.67$ is more than triple ours. That's by design — ZN targets fast disturbance rejection and is willing to overshoot to get it. Our recipe targets a smooth approach. Treat ZN as a starting point, then de-tune toward smooth: the hand-tuned gains are essentially ZN with the integral pulled way back and a hair off the proportional.

④ Real case when to reach for which

If your USV must reject a gusting crosswind now — a survey line where drift is the whole problem — start at ZN and live with the overshoot. If it's holding station for a sensor or threading a marina, take the smoother hand-tune. Either way you do the same measurement (raise $k_p$ to $K_u$, time $T_u$); the table is just a different opinion about how aggressive to be with what you measured.

6From the sim to your actual USV (ROS 2)

③ Code map every sim variable to a real signal

The controller you tuned doesn't change at all. What changes is where the numbers come from and where they go. Here is the exact mapping:

In the simOn the boat (ROS 2)
psiyaw from IMU / /odometry/filtered (already fused)
wrap(sp − psi)same — do it in your node, every cycle
DT = 0.05timer callback ~20–50 Hz, but use measured dt
rudder $\delta$rudder servo, or differential thrust: left = base+u, right = base−u
RUDDER_MAXyour actuator limits — clamp in software too
current disturbancereal current / wind — your I term's job

Two of these rows are landmines if you skim them. Measured dt: a timer "set to 20 Hz" does not fire at exactly 0.05 s — jitter, a busy CPU, or a dropped cycle changes the real interval, and both your I term ($\times dt$) and D term ($\div dt$) scale with it. Use the timestamp delta, not the nominal rate. Wrap every cycle: the angle-wrap bug (Case 8) is invisible until your test happens to cross north, then the boat turns 340° the long way around a dock. Wrap the error before it ever enters the PID.

heading_node.py Python · rclpy
import math, rclpy
from rclpy.node import Node
from std_msgs.msg import Float64
from nav_msgs.msg import Odometry
from pid_controller import PID            # the class from Ch 01

def wrap(a):                              # → (-pi, pi], used on EVERY error
    return (a + math.pi) % (2 * math.pi) - math.pi

class HeadingNode(Node):
    def __init__(self):
        super().__init__("heading_pid")
        # the gains you tuned in the sim — unchanged
        self.pid = PID(kp=2.0, ki=0.2, kd=3.0, output_limits=(-0.61, 0.61))  # ±35°
        self.setpoint = 0.0              # rad, from a waypoint/teleop topic
        self.last_t   = None            # for MEASURED dt
        self.armed    = False           # kill-switch gate (see §7)

        self.create_subscription(Odometry, "/odometry/filtered", self.on_odom, 10)
        self.cmd = self.create_publisher(Float64, "/rudder_cmd", 10)

    def on_odom(self, msg):
        # 1. yaw straight out of the fused estimate (quaternion → heading)
        q = msg.pose.pose.orientation
        psi = math.atan2(2*(q.w*q.z + q.x*q.y),
                         1 - 2*(q.y*q.y + q.z*q.z))

        # 2. MEASURED dt, not the nominal 0.05 s
        t  = self.get_clock().now().nanoseconds * 1e-9
        if self.last_t is None:
            self.last_t = t
            return                       # need two samples for a dt
        dt = t - self.last_t
        self.last_t = t
        if dt <= 0.0 or dt > 0.5:        # skip absurd gaps (dropped cycles)
            return

        # 3. wrap the error EVERY cycle, then run the same PID
        error = wrap(self.setpoint - psi)
        if not self.armed:               # kill switch open → no actuation
            self.pid.reset()             # keep the integral clean while idle
            return
        u = self.pid.update(error, dt)   # rad of rudder, already clamped

        self.cmd.publish(Float64(data=u))
⚠️ Clamp in software even though the servo clamps too

RUDDER_MAX is not just a courtesy to the hardware. If you let the PID command beyond the physical stop, the integral keeps winding against a limit it can't see — that's exactly the windup of Case 5 (a saturated rudder + a still-accumulating integral = donuts forever). Clamp the output and protect the integral (anti-windup, Ch 05). The servo's mechanical stop is your last line of defense, not your first.

7Before you put it in the water: safety

④ Real case the two rules that save your boat

Field-tuning order is identical to the sim: P → D → I, starting low. You do it on the water, but you start from the gains the sim handed you, so you're refining, not searching blind. Two non-negotiables before any autonomy test:

  1. A hardware kill switch. Not a software flag — a physical interlock that cuts actuation regardless of what the code thinks. An RC override channel or a relay on the thruster power. The first time your tuning is wrong (and it will be), this is the difference between a debrief and a swim.
  2. Call reset() whenever the controller is re-engaged. A stale integral from five minutes ago — accumulated while the boat sat at the dock, or while the kill switch was open — is a windup bug waiting to fire the instant you arm. On re-engage, zero the integral and clear prev_error so there's no phantom derivative kick from a heading that "jumped" while you weren't looking.
arming / re-engage handler Python
def on_kill_switch(self, engaged: bool):
    """Wired to the RC override / hardware interlock."""
    if engaged and not self.armed:
        self.pid.reset()        # <-- the rule: clean integral on re-engage
        self.last_t = None      # force a fresh dt; no derivative kick
    self.armed = engaged
    if not engaged:
        self.cmd.publish(Float64(data=0.0))   # rudder to neutral, hands off
🔑 Why reset() matters more on a boat than in the sim

In the sim the controller runs continuously from $t=0$. On the water it gets armed, disarmed for a manual correction, re-armed near a dock — and each disarm period is silent error the integral would have eaten if you'd let it. reset() on re-engage makes every autonomous segment start from a known-clean state. Pair it with the kill switch (which calls it for you) and you've closed the gap between "works in sim" and "works on the water."

8Your turn

Q1 Conceptual check
You're tuning a fresh boat. Why do we raise $k_p$ to oscillation and then add $k_d$, rather than tuning $k_d$ first or all three together?
  • $k_d$ has the largest effect, so it must be set first to stay in range.
  • D only acts on motion, so there's nothing for it to damp until P is driving the turn — and I lies about offset while the transient still rings.
  • Ziegler–Nichols requires the gains to be set in alphabetical order.
  • Setting all three at once is fine; the order is just convention.
Each term takes effect on a different timescale and depends on the one before it. P (fast) does the turn; D (faster) can only oppose change, so it needs P's transient to act on; I (slowest) should only see the steady offset that's left once the transient has settled. Tune them in the order they take effect: P → D → I.
Q2 Compute it
Ziegler–Nichols classic gives $k_i = 1.2\,K_u/T_u$. With the measured $K_u = 5$ and $T_u = 9$ s, what integral gain does ZN prescribe? (Round to 2 decimals.)
rad/(rad·s)
$k_i = 1.2 \times 5 / 9 = 6/9 = 0.667 \approx 0.67$. That's more than triple our hand-tuned $k_i=0.2$ — ZN runs the integral hot to reject disturbances fast, accepting overshoot. We de-tune it down for a smooth approach.
Q3 Design — why measured dt, and what breaks without it
Your ROS 2 timer is "set to 20 Hz" so you hardcode dt = 0.05. The CPU gets busy and a few callbacks fire at 0.08 s and 0.03 s instead. What goes wrong in the I and D terms, and how does measuring dt fix it?
Hint
Write the discrete I and D terms and notice that one multiplies by dt and the other divides by it. Then ask what happens when the real interval differs from the 0.05 you assumed.
Show one solution

The integral adds $e\cdot dt$ and the derivative computes $(e_n - e_{n-1})/dt$. Hardcoding dt=0.05 while the real interval is 0.08 s means the integral accumulates too little (you under-count the area, so I trims the current too slowly) and the derivative reports a swing rate that's too high by 0.08/0.05 = 1.6× (phantom damping, and noise amplified). At 0.03 s the errors flip the other way. The fix is to measure the actual interval from the message/clock timestamps — dt = t - self.last_t — so every term scales by the true elapsed time. As a bonus, you can reject absurd gaps (dt > 0.5 from a dropped cycle) instead of feeding a garbage derivative into the loop.

🔑 Chapter recap

Tune in order: raise $k_p$ to the ultimate gain $K_u\approx5$ (period $T_u\approx9$ s) and halve it; add $k_d\approx3$ until overshoot dies; add the smallest $k_i\approx0.2$ that erases the offset — landing on $k_p=2,\ k_i=0.2,\ k_d=3$. Ziegler–Nichols from the same $K_u,T_u$ gives a hotter $3.0 / 0.67 / 3.4$; use it as a starting point and de-tune toward smooth. Deploying to ROS 2 doesn't change the controller — it changes the plumbing: fused yaw from /odometry/filtered, wrap every cycle, use measured dt, clamp in software. And before the water: a hardware kill switch, and reset() on every re-engage. That's the whole course, on a real boat.
