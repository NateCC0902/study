I Control: The Trim Tab

Your PD controller holds course beautifully — and lies about it. It sits a couple of degrees off the setpoint and calls it done, because that offset is the only way P can keep the rudder pushed against the current. This chapter is about the one term that fixes that: the integral, the part of the controller that learns the disturbance and trims it out so the error can finally go to zero.

⏱ ~30 min🧭 Case 4⚡ Live sim∑ Integral term

1The leftover problem: PD holds course by staying wrong

① Intuition where we left off

We've built a good PD controller: $k_p=2$ gets us there, $k_d=3$ glides us in without overshoot. It's the parking-valet controller from Chapter 03. But it has a defect that only shows up when a persistent disturbance is present — a current, a steady crosswind, a trim imbalance. Recall the boat: the model says the current acts exactly like a 4° stuck rudder pushing the bow.

💡 The trim-tab analogy

On a real boat or a sailplane there's a small trim tab — a tiny secondary surface you set once so the main control surface can rest at neutral while still holding against a steady force. The integral term is the trim tab the autopilot adjusts for itself. It quietly winds in exactly enough bias to cancel the current, so the proportional term no longer has to fight it.

To hold a steady heading, the net yaw moment must be zero. The current is a constant $+4°$ of equivalent rudder, so something in the controller has to hold a steady $-4°$ to cancel it. A pure P controller can only output $k_p e$ — and the only way it produces $-4°$ of rudder is to sit at a non-zero error:

$$ k_p\,e_{ss} = -4° \quad\Longrightarrow\quad e_{ss} = \frac{-4°}{k_p} $$

At $k_p=2$ that's $e_{ss}=-2°$: the boat settles at $92°$ when you asked for $90°$. D doesn't help — once you're sitting still, the derivative is zero, so D contributes nothing. PD holds course by staying wrong. That isn't a tuning failure; it's structural. At zero error a PD controller outputs zero rudder, and zero rudder loses to the current.

⚠️ You can't just crank $k_p$

The tempting fix — shrink the offset by raising $k_p$ — runs straight into Chapter 01–02's wall: this boat goes unstable near $k_p\approx5$ (the ultimate gain $K_u$). With usable gains the offset is permanent. We need a fundamentally different mechanism, not a bigger spring.

2What the integral is actually for

① Intuition the controller grows a memory

The proportional term reacts to where you are right now. The derivative term reacts to where you're going. Neither has any memory of the past. The integral term is the controller's memory: it accumulates error over time. If a small error lingers, the integral keeps growing, leaning harder and harder, until it has built up enough output to push the error to zero — and then it stays there, holding the bias even when the error is gone.

flowchart LR E["error e = psi_set - psi"] --> P["P: kp e (now)"] E --> D["D: kd de/dt (trend)"] E -->|"sum e*dt over time"| I["I: ki integral (history)"] P --> U["rudder u"] D --> U I --> U U --> B["boat"] C["current d = +4 deg"] --> B B -->|"heading psi"| E
② Math why this forces zero error

Here is the argument that the integral makes steady-state error exactly zero, not just small. Suppose the closed loop settles — every signal goes constant. The integral term is $i = k_i \int_0^t e\,d\tau$. For $i$ to be constant, its rate of change must be zero:

$$ \frac{di}{dt} = k_i\, e(t) = 0 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad e(t)=0 $$

That's the whole trick. The only way the integral can stop growing is if the error is zero. So as long as any error persists, the integral keeps moving the rudder; it can only come to rest when it has driven the error to nothing. And crucially, when it comes to rest it doesn't unwind — it holds whatever bias it has accumulated. That held value is the trim that cancels the current.

∑ Where the trim ends up

At the new steady state the boat is back on $90°$, so $e=0$ and therefore $P=0$ and $D=0$. The current still pushes with $+4°$, so the rudder must still hold $-4°$ — and the only term left to supply it is I. The integral has ramped to exactly $k_i\,\text{integral} = -4°$ and parked there. The integral has learned the current.

3Watch it learn the current

④ Real case Case 4, live

This is Case 4 from the notes, running for real. The boat starts already settled on $90°$. At $t=20$ s the current switches on — a step disturbance equal to that $4°$ stuck rudder. Watch the heading panel and the rudder panel together.

🧭 PID — disturbance switches on at t=20 s, I trims it out
🔑 What to drag, what to watch

Set $k_p=2$, $k_d=3$ and start with $k_i=0$ (pure PD). At $t=20$ s the current kicks in and the boat bends to about $92°$ and stays there — that's the structural offset from §1. Now bring $k_i$ up to $0.2$ and re-run: after the kick the heading dips to roughly $91.7°$, then crawls back to exactly $90.0°$ over about $30$ s. In the rudder panel you can literally watch the steady rudder bias slide down to $-4°$ — that's the integral filling in the trim while the proportional contribution relaxes back to zero.

Two numbers to read straight off the plot once $k_i=0.2$:

  • The recovery is slow on purpose — about $30$ s. The integral is supposed to respond to things that persist, not to every passing wave. Slow is correct here.
  • The PD trace never recovers — it just sits at $92°$ forever. Same boat, same disturbance; the only difference is whether there is a memory term carrying the trim.
⚠️ Now turn $k_i$ up too far

Crank $k_i$ to $2$ and re-run. The boat overshoots the recovery and weaves slowly back and forth across $90°$ for ages before it settles. That's the integral drawing jumpy conclusions from a short memory — it builds bias faster than the slow hull can respond, overshoots, then has to unwind. This previews §4: $k_i$ must be the slowest thing in the loop.

4Why $k_i$ must be small — the slowest thing in the loop

① Intuition memory vs. reflex

Think of the three terms as three different timescales of decision-making. P is a reflex — instant reaction to the present. D is anticipation — reaction to the immediate trend. I is judgment — a conclusion drawn from accumulated evidence. Judgment should be the last to change its mind, not the first. A controller whose memory term twitches as fast as its reflex is a controller that panics over noise and short transients.

🔑 The integral should be the slowest thing in the loop

Big $k_i$ = jumpy conclusions from short memory = overshoot and slow weaving. Small $k_i$ = patient, only leans on errors that truly persist. The integral's whole value is that it ignores the transient and learns the steady bias. Make it fast and you destroy the one property you added it for.

② Math the integral adds lag

There's a hard reason behind the rule of thumb, not just taste. The integral introduces phase lag: its output trails the error, because it's an accumulation of the past. In the frequency view, an integrator contributes $-90°$ of phase. Phase lag is exactly what eats your stability margin — it's the same mechanism that makes high $k_p$ oscillate (the servo's lag, Chapter 02). So the integral is, by construction, a destabilizing term. You buy zero steady-state error and you pay in phase margin. Keep $k_i$ small and you keep the bill small.

$$ G_I(j\omega) = \frac{k_i}{j\omega} \quad\Rightarrow\quad \angle G_I = -90°,\qquad |G_I| \propto \frac{1}{\omega} $$

Notice the magnitude: $|G_I|\propto 1/\omega$. The integrator has huge gain at low frequency (which is why it crushes the constant offset) and small gain at high frequency (which is why it sensibly ignores fast wave-chop). That low-frequency authority is the feature; the $-90°$ of phase is the price. The tuning recipe — "add the smallest $k_i$ that removes the offset in reasonable time" — is just balancing those two.

5The integral in code

③ Code read every line

The integral term is two lines, but both lines matter. The accumulator persists across calls — that's what makes it a memory and not a formula. And the gain $k_i$ multiplies the whole accumulated sum, not the current error.

control step (per dt) Python
error      = wrap(setpoint - heading)        # rad, ALWAYS wrapped (Ch 07)
integral  += error * dt                       # rad*s, PERSISTS across calls
derivative = (error - prev_error) / dt        # rad/s

p = kp * error
i = ki * integral                             # <- the trim tab: learns the bias
d = kd * derivative

u = p + i + d                                  # rad of rudder
u = clamp(u, -RUDDER_MAX, +RUDDER_MAX)
prev_error = error

Trace what happens to those three contributions across the Case 4 experiment. Before $t=20$ s the boat is on $90°$, error is zero: P=0, I=0 (nothing accumulated), D=0, rudder rests at $0°$. The current switches on; the bow drifts, error grows, P pushes back and arrests it at $\approx92°$. Now integral += error*dt starts ticking up every step because the error is non-zero and staying non-zero. As the integral grows, $i = k_i\,\text{integral}$ takes over the load, the rudder bias slides toward $-4°$, the error shrinks back toward zero, P fades out — and the integral, finding $e=0$, stops growing exactly where it is. Handover complete.

pid_controller.py Python
class PID:
    def __init__(self, kp, ki, kd, output_limits=(-1.0, 1.0)):
        self.kp, self.ki, self.kd = kp, ki, kd
        self.out_min, self.out_max = output_limits
        self.integral = 0.0
        self.prev_error = None

    def reset(self):
        """Call when re-engaging: a stale integral is a windup bug (Ch 05)."""
        self.integral = 0.0
        self.prev_error = None

    def update(self, error, dt):
        p = self.kp * error
        self.integral += error * dt                 # the memory grows here
        i = self.ki * self.integral
        d = 0.0 if self.prev_error is None else self.kd * (error - self.prev_error) / dt
        self.prev_error = error
        u = p + i + d
        return max(self.out_min, min(self.out_max, u))   # clamp
🛠️ The clamp hides a trap

Notice the integral keeps accumulating even when the output is clamped to the rudder stop. For the gentle Case 4 disturbance that's harmless — the rudder never saturates. But for a big maneuver it is exactly how integral windup (Chapter 05) is born: the memory fills up against a wall it can't see. We'll meet that bug head-on next chapter. For now, savour the integral doing its honest job.

Right now P is still doing most of the work ($-3.0°$) and I has only built up $-2.3°$. But every step the integral grows a little more negative while P shrinks as the error closes. Fast-forward to steady state: error $\to0$ so P $\to0$ and D $\to0$, while the integral has ramped until $k_i\,\text{integral} = -4°$ all on its own. The total rudder stays near $-4°$ throughout — the composition just shifts from P-supplied to I-supplied. That shift is the trim tab taking the load off your hands.

7Your turn

Q1 Conceptual check
Why does adding the integral term drive the steady-state heading error to exactly zero, rather than merely shrinking it like a bigger $k_p$ would?
  • Because $k_i$ is larger than $k_p$, so it dominates the output.
  • At steady state the integral stops changing only if the error is zero, so the loop can rest only when $e=0$.
  • Because the integral cancels the derivative term once the boat stops moving.
  • Because the current disturbance fades away on its own after a while.
A constant integral requires $\tfrac{di}{dt}=k_i e=0$, which forces $e=0$. The held integral value then supplies the $-4°$ trim that cancels the current, while P and D both go to zero. A bigger $k_p$ only divides the offset by $k_p$ ($e_{ss}=-4°/k_p$) — it never reaches zero, and you run out of usable $k_p$ before it gets close.
Q2 Compute it
With a pure PD controller ($k_i=0$), $k_p=2$, and a current equal to a $4°$ stuck rudder, what is the magnitude of the steady-state heading offset, in degrees? (Hint: $k_p e_{ss}=-4°$.)
°
$e_{ss} = -4°/k_p = -4°/2 = -2°$, so the boat settles $2°$ off — at $92°$ when commanded to $90°$. To get $-4°$ of rudder out of a P term, you must sit at a $-2°$ error. Adding $k_i$ removes it because the integral, not the error, ends up carrying the trim.
Q3 Reasoning — why slow is the feature
In the sim with $k_i=0.2$, recovery after the $t=20$ s kick takes about $30$ s. Raising $k_i$ to $2$ makes it overshoot and weave. Explain, in terms of what the integral represents, why the slow version is the correct design — and what physical reason makes $k_i=2$ misbehave.
Hint
The integral is a memory of accumulated error. What kinds of error should it respond to, and what kinds should it ignore? And recall that an integrator adds $-90°$ of phase lag to the loop.
Show one solution

The integral exists to cancel disturbances that persist — a steady current, a trim imbalance — not transient ones like a single wave. A slow integral averages over time and only leans on errors that stay non-zero, which is exactly what a current is. That patience is the property we added it for.

Crank $k_i$ to $2$ and two things break. First, the memory now reacts to short transients it should ignore, so it builds bias before the slow hull (time constant $T=2.5$ s) can respond — it overshoots the trim, then has to unwind, producing the slow weave. Second, the integrator contributes $-90°$ of phase lag; pump up $k_i$ and you spend stability margin, pushing the loop toward oscillation. The cure is the recipe: add the smallest $k_i$ that removes the offset in acceptable time, then stop. The integral should be the slowest thing in the loop.

🔑 Chapter recap

A PD controller holds course by staying wrong: to push $-4°$ of rudder against the current, P must sit at $e_{ss}=-4°/k_p$, a structural offset you can't tune away with usable gains. The integral fixes it by becoming the controller's memory — it accumulates error and can only stop growing when $e=0$, so it drives the steady-state error to exactly zero and parks at the $-4°$ trim the current demands. In the sim, the disturbance steps on at $t=20$ s; PD bends and stays bent, PID dips and recovers in ~$30$ s. That slowness is the feature, and it's why $k_i$ must be the smallest, slowest term in the loop — every bit of integral you add buys low-frequency authority but spends phase margin.
