P Control: The Spring & Its Offset

Turn on exactly one term — proportional — and the boat becomes a mass on a spring. We sweep $k_p$ to watch it go from sluggish to ringing forever, then prove why P alone can never sit on the setpoint when a current pushes the bow. Two cases, one stubborn lesson: more gain is not the answer.

⏱ ~30 min🪝 The spring analogy⚡ Live kp sweep📐 e_ss = −4°/kp

1P alone: the boat becomes a spring

① Intuition find the picture first

Strip the controller down to one term. No integral, no derivative — just $u = k_p\,e$. The rudder command is proportional to how far off you are: way off, hard over; nearly there, barely a nudge; on the setpoint, zero rudder. That last clause is innocent-looking and it's the villain of section 5.

💡 P is a spring

A spring pulls back with a force proportional to how far you've stretched it: $F = -kx$. A P controller pushes the rudder proportional to how far the heading is stretched from the setpoint: $u = k_p\,e$. Same law, same personality — including the bad parts. Hang a mass on a spring with little friction and it oscillates. Our "mass" is the hull's yaw inertia; our "friction" is hydrodynamic yaw damping. Stiffen the spring enough and the friction can't keep up.

So before we touch a slider, predict the shape: a stiffer spring (bigger $k_p$) snaps toward the target faster, but a fast-moving mass carries momentum past it — overshoot — and a weakly-damped spring rings on the way to rest. Crank the stiffness high enough and it never comes to rest at all.

flowchart LR E["error e = ψ_set − ψ"] -->|"× kp"| U["rudder u = kp·e"] U -->|"plant (K, T)"| R["yaw rate r"] R -->|"∫ dt"| P["heading ψ"] P -->|"feedback"| E
🔑 The pattern to memorize

More $k_p$ = faster response, less leftover offset, more ringing — until it never stops ringing. Everything in this chapter is a consequence of that one sentence.

2The kp sweep: from sluggish to never-settling

④ Real case Case 1 from the notes

The experiment is simple: $u = k_p e$, command a turn from 0° to 90°, and sweep the gain. Here's what the boat does at four values — read it like a personality test for $k_p$.

$k_p$BehaviourSettles at
0.5sluggish; big sloppy overshoot~98° (big offset → §4)
2brisk; rings once; usable~92°
5never settles — ±16° oscillation, forever— (this is $K_u$)
8bigger, faster oscillation±25°, forever

That $k_p=5$ row is special. The boat enters a sustained oscillation that neither grows nor decays — a clean ±16° wobble with period $T_u\approx 9$ s. This is the ultimate gain $K_u$: the stiffness at which the spring's ringing exactly balances the hull's damping. It's not a curiosity — it's the single most useful number for tuning this boat (we cash it in for real gains in Ch 08).

⚠️ "I'll just crank kp" is a trap

Every instinct says: offset too big? raise the gain. And it does shrink the offset (§4). But $k_p\ge 5$ oscillates without end. You are squeezed between a steady-state error you can't fully kill and an oscillation you can't tolerate. P alone cannot win this — which is exactly why I and D exist.

Don't take my word for the four rows — go drive it. Pull $k_p$ up slowly and find the value where the wobble stops shrinking and stops growing. That crossover is $K_u$.

🧭 P-only heading hold (sweep kp; turn on the current)
🛠️ What to drag, what to watch

Sweep $k_p$ low → high. Near 0.5 the boat crawls in and parks well short — note where it stops, not just that it's slow. Around 2 it arrives smartly with one small ring. Push toward 5 and the oscillation locks into a steady ±16° forever — that's $K_u$; time one full cycle and you'll get $T_u\approx 9$ s. Past 5 it gets worse, not better. Then turn the current on and re-run: every settling point shifts: that's §4's offset, live.

3Why it oscillates: spring meets lag

② Math where the ringing comes from

Close the P loop on the Nomoto plant from Ch 01 and the dynamics become second-order. Start from the plant and substitute the control law $u = k_p e$ with $e = \psi_\text{set}-\psi$ (no disturbance for now):

$$ T\,\ddot\psi + \dot\psi = K\,u = K\,k_p\,(\psi_\text{set} - \psi) $$

Rearranged, that's the equation of a damped harmonic oscillator driven toward $\psi_\text{set}$:

$$ T\,\ddot\psi + \underbrace{1\cdot\dot\psi}_{\text{damping}} + \underbrace{K k_p\,\psi}_{\text{spring}} = K k_p\,\psi_\text{set} $$

Read off the two knobs. The spring stiffness is $K k_p$ — it grows with the gain you set. The damping coefficient is fixed at $1$ (it's the hull's own yaw damping, baked into the plant). The natural frequency and damping ratio fall straight out:

$$ \omega_n = \sqrt{\frac{K k_p}{T}}, \qquad \zeta = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{K k_p\,T}} $$

Now the whole chapter is in one inequality. Crank $k_p$ and $\zeta$ drops — more stiffness, same friction, so proportionally less damping. The mass swings harder past the target each time. With $K=0.6$ and $T=2.5$, $\zeta$ falls below the comfortable range right where the notes say it does: around $k_p\approx 2$ you get a single tidy overshoot, and by $k_p=5$ the lag in the loop has eaten the last of the effective damping and you ring forever.

∑ Honest caveat (full story in Ch 09)

A bare first-order Nomoto boat with a perfect rudder is technically too well-behaved — on paper, even enormous $k_p$ settles. Real boats oscillate because the actuator lags: our servo slews at 25 °/s and can't follow a command that flips rail-to-rail. That phase lag is the missing ingredient that turns "less damped" into "undamped" at $K_u\approx 5$. The sim has the slew limit built in, so it oscillates like the real thing.

4The structural offset: P can't beat a current

① Intuition Case 2 from the notes

Now turn on the current — the constant push that acts like 4° of stuck rudder. Run the P boat to a heading and look closely at where it parks. It won't be on the setpoint. And no amount of waiting fixes it: the error sits there, steady and stubborn. This isn't sloppiness or a slow transient — it's structural, and three lines of algebra tell you exactly how big it is.

② Math do this once, it's 3 lines

To hold a steady heading, the net yaw moment must be zero. The current pushes like +4° of rudder, so the controller's rudder must hold −4° to cancel it — otherwise the boat keeps turning. But a P controller can only ever output $k_p\,e$. Set that equal to the rudder the boat needs:

$$ k_p\,e_{ss} = -4° \qquad\Longrightarrow\qquad \boxed{\,e_{ss} = -\frac{4°}{k_p}\,} $$

There it is. To produce the −4° of holding rudder, the P term requires a nonzero error to multiply — so the boat settles off-target by exactly $4°/k_p$. Plug in gains:

$k_p$offset $|e_{ss}|$settles at (target 90°)
14°94°
22°92°
41°91°
🔑 Why this is structural, not a bug

At zero error a P controller outputs zero rudder — and zero rudder loses to the current. So the only way P can hold any nonzero rudder is to keep a nonzero error alive to multiply by $k_p$. The offset isn't a tuning mistake; it's the math of the term. Yes, bigger $k_p$ shrinks it ($4°/k_p \to 0$) — but §2 already capped you: $k_p\ge 5$ oscillates. With usable gains you're stuck a degree or two off, forever.

④ Real case this is everywhere

The same droop appears in every proportional-only loop fighting a constant load: a cruise-control car that sags a few mph on a long uphill, a thermostat that holds a chilly 19° when it's freezing outside, an op-amp follower with finite gain. The cure is always the same — something must hold the actuator while the error is zero. That something is the integral term, and meeting it is Ch 05's whole job.

💡 Tease for I

Imagine a term that remembers "I've been 2° short for a while now" and slowly leans on the rudder until the error is gone — then keeps holding that lean even at zero error. That's integral: a self-trimming tab that learns the current. P can't do it because P has no memory; it only knows where you are right now.

5P-only, in code

③ Code read every line

Here's the entire P controller — the Ch 01 PID class with two gains zeroed. I'm showing it explicitly so the offset is undeniable: trace the control step at $e=0$ and the output is exactly zero rudder, no matter the disturbance.

p_only.py Python
import math

def wrap(angle):                     # → (-pi, pi]; matters once you cross north (Ch 07)
    return (angle + math.pi) % (2 * math.pi) - math.pi

class PController:
    """The whole P controller. ki = kd = 0 — no memory, no lookahead."""
    def __init__(self, kp, rudder_max=math.radians(35)):
        self.kp = kp
        self.rudder_max = rudder_max

    def update(self, setpoint, heading):
        error = wrap(setpoint - heading)          # rad
        u = self.kp * error                        # the ENTIRE control law
        # NOTE: when error == 0, u == 0. Zero rudder loses to the current.
        return max(-self.rudder_max, min(self.rudder_max, u))

And the steady-state check — the simulator equivalent of staring at where the boat parks. We solve for the heading at which everything stops moving with the current switched on:

offset_check.py Python
import math

K, T   = 0.6, 2.5                  # Nomoto plant (Ch 01)
current = math.radians(4.0)        # disturbance ≡ +4° of stuck rudder
setpoint = math.radians(90.0)

for kp in (1.0, 2.0, 4.0):
    # Steady state: yaw rate r = 0  ⇒  net rudder (u + current) = 0  ⇒  u = -current.
    # P law: u = kp * e_ss,  and e_ss = setpoint - heading_ss.
    #   kp * e_ss = -current  ⇒  e_ss = -current / kp
    e_ss   = -current / kp
    settle = setpoint - e_ss                       # where the bow actually parks
    print(f"kp={kp:>3}:  offset = {math.degrees(e_ss):+5.1f}°"
          f"   settles at {math.degrees(settle):5.1f}°")

# kp=1.0:  offset =  -4.0°   settles at  94.0°
# kp=2.0:  offset =  -2.0°   settles at  92.0°
# kp=4.0:  offset =  -1.0°   settles at  91.0°
🛠️ Sign convention, so you don't trip

The error is $e = \psi_\text{set}-\psi$. A current that pushes the bow up in heading needs the controller to hold negative rudder to cancel it, so $e_{ss}$ comes out negative — the boat settles a touch past the setpoint, at 94° not 86°. The magnitude $4°/k_p$ is what you tune against; the sign just tells you which way it droops.

6Two failures, one root cause

Step back. We met two distinct misbehaviours, and they are not really two problems — they're the same limitation wearing different clothes.

  1. Oscillation (no current): push $k_p$ up and the spring out-stiffens the damping. Ringing, then a never-ending wobble at $K_u\approx 5$.
  2. Offset (with current): a P controller outputs zero rudder at zero error, so it can't hold the −4° trim a steady current demands. It settles $4°/k_p$ short.
⚠️ The squeeze

Each failure points at the opposite gain. Offset wants $k_p$ high (shrink $4°/k_p$). Oscillation wants $k_p$ low (stay under $K_u$). One knob can't satisfy both, because $k_p$ alone has no memory and no anticipation. The fix isn't a better $k_p$ — it's two more terms: D to add damping so you can run more gain (Ch 03), and I to kill the offset without any gain at all (Ch 05).

7Your turn

Q1 Conceptual check
With the current on, a P-only boat settles a couple of degrees off the setpoint and stays there. Why can't it ever reach the setpoint exactly?
  • The gain $k_p$ is set too low; a high enough $k_p$ would reach it exactly.
  • At zero error a P controller outputs zero rudder, but the current needs −4° of held rudder — so a nonzero error must remain to produce it.
  • The rudder saturates at ±35° and can't push hard enough.
  • The plant is unstable, so it can never settle anywhere.
Holding a steady heading needs net zero yaw moment, i.e. rudder $=-4°$. P can only make rudder via $k_p\,e$, so it needs $e_{ss}=-4°/k_p \ne 0$. Raising $k_p$ shrinks the offset toward zero but never reaches it — and you can't raise it far anyway, since $k_p\ge 5$ oscillates. The offset is structural; killing it needs the integral term.
Q2 Compute it
A P-only boat with $k_p = 2.5$ is commanded to 90° with the 4° current on. Using $e_{ss} = -4°/k_p$, at what heading (in degrees) does it actually settle?
°
$e_{ss} = -4°/2.5 = -1.6°$. Since $e = \psi_\text{set}-\psi$, a negative error means $\psi$ overshoots the setpoint: $\psi_{ss} = 90° - e_{ss} = 90° - (-1.6°) = 91.6°$. Note $k_p=2.5$ is the high end of the usable range — below $K_u\approx 5$, so no oscillation — and even there the offset is still 1.6°.
Q3 Derivation — why higher $k_p$ rings more
Starting from the closed-loop equation $T\ddot\psi + \dot\psi + K k_p\,\psi = K k_p\,\psi_\text{set}$, show how the damping ratio $\zeta$ depends on $k_p$, and explain in one line why cranking the gain makes the boat oscillate.
Hint
Compare term-by-term with the standard second-order form $\ddot\psi + 2\zeta\omega_n\dot\psi + \omega_n^2\psi = \omega_n^2\psi_\text{set}$. First divide the whole equation by $T$ so the $\ddot\psi$ coefficient is 1.
Show one solution

Divide by $T$: $\ddot\psi + \dfrac{1}{T}\dot\psi + \dfrac{K k_p}{T}\psi = \dfrac{K k_p}{T}\psi_\text{set}$. Matching the standard form gives $\omega_n^2 = \dfrac{K k_p}{T}$, so $\omega_n = \sqrt{\dfrac{K k_p}{T}}$, and $2\zeta\omega_n = \dfrac{1}{T}$, so

$$\zeta = \frac{1}{2\omega_n T} = \frac{1}{2T}\sqrt{\frac{T}{K k_p}} = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{K k_p\,T}}.$$

One line: $\zeta \propto 1/\sqrt{k_p}$ — the spring stiffens with $k_p$ but the damping is fixed by the hull, so raising the gain drops the damping ratio and the boat rings harder. (On the ideal model $\zeta$ never quite hits zero; in §3's caveat, real actuator lag is what finally pushes effective $\zeta$ to zero at $K_u$, producing the sustained ±16° oscillation.)

🔑 Chapter recap

P alone is a spring: $u=k_p e$. Closed on the boat it's a damped oscillator with $\zeta = 1/(2\sqrt{K k_p T})$ — so more $k_p$ means faster response but less damping, ringing harder until it never settles at $K_u\approx 5$ (period $T_u\approx 9$ s). Worse, against a steady current P can't sit on the setpoint at all: zero error gives zero rudder, so it droops by the structural offset $e_{ss}=-4°/k_p$. The two failures pull $k_p$ in opposite directions — proof that one term isn't enough. Next: add D to buy back damping so you can finally run a useful gain.
