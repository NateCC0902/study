Derivative Kick & Sensor Noise

The D term is the most useful and the most fragile part of the loop. Two everyday events turn it into a liability: moving the setpoint (a ~600° rudder spike that achieves nothing) and compass noise (a servo grinding itself to death). Both have one-line fixes — but only if you understand what the derivative is actually differentiating.

⏱ ~30 min🧭 Derivative-on-measurement🔇 Low-pass filter⚡ Live sim

1What the derivative is actually differentiating

① Intuition name the input before you blame the output

The textbook D term is $k_d\,\dfrac{de}{dt}$ — the rate of change of the error. That phrasing hides a trap. Error is $e = \psi_\text{set} - \psi$, so the error can change for two completely different reasons: the boat moved ($\psi$ changed), or you moved the goalposts ($\psi_\text{set}$ changed). The derivative can't tell these apart; it just sees $e$ jump and reacts.

💡 The whole chapter in one line

D was hired to oppose the boat's swing. Differentiating the error makes it also react to your commands and to sensor lies. The fixes both come down to: differentiate the right signal, and don't trust it too literally.

When the setpoint sits still, $\dot e = -\dot\psi$ and the two formulations are identical — D opposes the boat's turning rate, exactly as designed (re-read Chapter 03's worked example: P and D cancelling on the approach is damping). The trouble starts the instant the setpoint moves or the measurement gets noisy. We meet both failures, then fix both.

② Math two signals, one sign flip apart

Write the discrete derivative both ways and the relationship is exact:

$$ \underbrace{\frac{e_n - e_{n-1}}{dt}}_{\text{D-on-error}} \;=\; \frac{(\psi_{\text{set},n}-\psi_n)-(\psi_{\text{set},n-1}-\psi_{n-1})}{dt} \;=\; \underbrace{-\frac{\psi_n - \psi_{n-1}}{dt}}_{\text{D-on-measurement}} \;+\; \frac{\Delta\psi_\text{set}}{dt} $$

They differ by exactly one term: $\Delta\psi_\text{set}/dt$, the rate of change of your command. Hold the setpoint constant and that term is zero — the two are identical. Step the setpoint and that term explodes, because $dt = 0.05$ s is tiny and a step is, by definition, an instantaneous change. That extra term is the derivative kick.

2Case 6 — the derivative kick

④ Real case a new waypoint arrives

The boat is cruising happily at 90°. A new waypoint comes in and your mission code does the obvious thing: setpoint = 100. The heading hasn't moved a millimetre — but the error just jumped 10° in a single 0.05 s sample. The derivative-of-error sees that as a screaming turn rate:

$$ \dot e = \frac{10°}{0.05\text{ s}} = 200\ \tfrac{\circ}{\text{s}} = 3.49\ \tfrac{\text{rad}}{\text{s}}, \qquad D = k_d\,\dot e = 3 \times 3.49 \approx 10.5\text{ rad} \approx \mathbf{600°\ of\ rudder} $$
⚠️ 600° of commanded rudder on a ±35° actuator

For one sample the command pegs hard against the +35° stop, then collapses back to normal the next sample (the kick lasts exactly one step — after that $\Delta\psi_\text{set}=0$ again). The boat barely notices a one-sample twitch, but the servo gets slammed rail-to-rail for nothing. The boat didn't start swinging — your setpoint moved. D reacted to a command as if it were a disturbance.

This is not a contrived edge case. Every waypoint hand-off, every operator nudge of the heading dial, every mission-planner segment transition steps the setpoint. With D-on-error you get a rudder spike on every single one.

③ Code the one-line fix

Differentiate the measurement instead of the error. From the §1 identity, when the setpoint is constant the two signals are numerically equal — so you lose nothing in normal operation — but the measurement simply does not contain the setpoint step, so the kick can never appear.

pid_controller.py — inside update() Python
# BEFORE — derivative of error: kicks when the setpoint moves
derivative = (error - self.prev_error) / dt

# AFTER — derivative of measurement: blind to setpoint steps
# d(e)/dt = -d(psi)/dt when setpoint is constant, so flip the sign
derivative = -(measurement - self.prev_measurement) / dt
self.prev_measurement = measurement      # remember psi, not e
🛠️ The sign flip is not optional

$e = \psi_\text{set} - \psi$, so $\dfrac{de}{dt} = -\dfrac{d\psi}{dt}$ when $\psi_\text{set}$ is constant. Forget the minus sign and D becomes positive feedback on the swing — it pushes the boat toward the way it's already turning. That's not a soft bug; it's an oscillator. Keep the negative.

3See both failures live

① Intuition break it, then fix it, with your thumb

This is the same boat from Chapter 01 (Nomoto, $K=0.6$, $T=2.5$ s), running the tuned gains $k_p=2,\ k_i=0.2,\ k_d=3$. At $t=30$ s the setpoint bumps to a new heading, and you can dial compass noise up and down. Two toggles let you turn each fix on and off independently — so you can watch the bug, then watch it disappear.

🧭 Setpoint bump at t=30 s + adjustable noise; toggle D-on-measurement and D low-pass
🔑 What to drag, and what to look for

1. The kick. Set noise to 0. With D-on-measurement OFF, watch the rudder panel at $t=30$ s — the command spikes to the +35° rail for one sample on the setpoint bump. Toggle D-on-measurement ON: the spike vanishes, while the heading response to the bump is unchanged. That's Case 6.

2. The chatter. Now crank the noise slider up (toward σ = 0.5°). With the D low-pass OFF, the rudder command turns into white-noise hash slamming the stops, and the servo trace grinds at its slew limit. Toggle the low-pass ON (α ≈ 0.85): the hash collapses to a fraction of its size. Look closely and the heading damps a hair more sluggishly — that's the lag the filter costs you. That's Case 7.

4Case 7 — noise, the derivative's other dark side

④ Real case a real compass, not a perfect one

You fixed the kick. Now you swap the perfect simulator heading for a real magnetometer, which reads with about $\sigma = 0.5°$ of noise per sample. The boat is dead steady on course — but D is differentiating, and the derivative of noise is brutal. Two adjacent independent readings differ by about $\sigma\sqrt2$ on average, divided by the tiny timestep:

$$ \text{derivative noise} \approx \frac{\sigma\sqrt2}{dt} = \frac{0.5°\times 1.414}{0.05\text{ s}} \approx 14\ \tfrac{\circ}{\text{s}} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad k_d \times 14\tfrac{\circ}{\text{s}} = 3 \times 14 \approx \mathbf{42°\ of\ rudder\ garbage} $$
⚠️ The boat is fine. Your hardware is dying.

Here's the cruel part: the command panel is white noise slamming the ±35° stops, the servo grinds nonstop at its 25 °/s slew limit — and the heading holds course just fine. The plant low-passes the garbage (it's a sluggish hull; ±42° of 10 Hz hash averages out). So the loop "works." Meanwhile your servo wears out in a week and your battery drains in an hour. This is a bug you find with an ammeter, not a heading plot.

② Math why differentiation amplifies high frequencies

It's not bad luck — it's what differentiation does. A sinusoid $\sin(\omega t)$ differentiated becomes $\omega\cos(\omega t)$: the amplitude is multiplied by the frequency $\omega$. Slow signals (the boat's actual swing, fractions of a Hz) barely change; fast signals (sensor noise, near the Nyquist frequency of 10 Hz) get multiplied by a huge $\omega$. The derivative is a high-pass filter, and noise lives precisely up where the gain is highest. That's structural — you can't tune it away with $k_d$ without also gutting your damping.

③ Code low-pass the derivative

The fix is to put a first-order low-pass (an exponential moving average) on the derivative after you compute it. Each step blends mostly the old filtered value with a little of the new raw one:

pid_controller.py — filtered derivative-on-measurement Python
ALPHA = 0.85     # 0 = no filter (raw), 1 = frozen (infinite lag)

# raw derivative of the MEASUREMENT (Case 6 fix already applied)
d_raw = -(measurement - self.prev_measurement) / dt
self.prev_measurement = measurement

# Case 7 fix: exponential low-pass on the derivative
self.d_filt = ALPHA * self.d_filt + (1.0 - ALPHA) * d_raw
derivative  = self.d_filt           # feed THIS into k_d, not d_raw

With $\alpha = 0.85$ each new noisy sample contributes only $1-\alpha = 0.15$ of its value, and uncorrelated noise averages toward zero across the blend. The math, for white noise of standard deviation $\sigma_d$ fed into an EMA:

$$ \sigma_\text{out} = \sigma_d\sqrt{\frac{1-\alpha}{1+\alpha}} = \sigma_d\sqrt{\frac{0.15}{1.85}} \approx 0.285\,\sigma_d $$

So the derivative chatter drops to roughly 28% of its raw size — the ±42° hash becomes about ±12°, and the servo stops thrashing.

② Math nothing is free — the lag trade-off

The filter doesn't only attenuate noise; it delays the derivative. An EMA with coefficient $\alpha$ and step $dt$ has an effective time constant

$$ \tau_\text{filt} = \frac{\alpha}{1-\alpha}\,dt = \frac{0.85}{0.15}\times 0.05\text{ s} \approx 0.28\text{ s} $$

That 0.28 s of lag is added to the D signal, so D reacts to the boat's swing a fraction of a second late. Late damping is weaker damping — push $\alpha$ too high and you re-introduce the overshoot D was supposed to kill. There's no escaping it: more noise rejection costs more phase lag, and phase lag is exactly what destabilises a loop (Chapter 09). $\alpha \approx 0.85$ is the sweet spot for this boat — enough to tame $\sigma=0.5°$, little enough that the damping barely notices.

🛰️ The real-world escape hatch: fix it upstream

The honest answer to heading noise isn't a fatter D filter — it's a better measurement. A gyro is quiet and fast but drifts; a compass is noisy but absolute. Fuse them (complementary filter, or an EKF) and you get a heading estimate that's both smooth and drift-free, so D has clean data to differentiate. That's what the IMU fusion on your flight controller already does, and why Chapter 01 told you to take heading from /odometry/filtered rather than the raw magnetometer. Filter the derivative as a backstop; fuse the sensor as the real fix.

5Both fixes, in one honest update()

③ Code the derivative block, finished

Here is the derivative half of update() with both fixes applied — measurement-derivative for the kick, EMA for the noise. Note the two new pieces of persistent state: prev_measurement (we remember $\psi$, not $e$, now) and d_filt (the filter's memory). Both must survive across calls, and both must be cleared in reset().

pid_controller.py Python
class PID:
    def __init__(self, kp, ki, kd, alpha=0.85, output_limits=(-1.0, 1.0)):
        self.kp, self.ki, self.kd = kp, ki, kd
        self.alpha = alpha                      # derivative low-pass coefficient
        self.out_min, self.out_max = output_limits
        self.integral = 0.0
        self.prev_measurement = None            # we differentiate psi now, not e
        self.d_filt = 0.0                       # EMA memory for the derivative

    def reset(self):
        self.integral = 0.0
        self.prev_measurement = None
        self.d_filt = 0.0

    def update(self, error, measurement, dt):
        """error = wrap(setpoint - measurement); measurement = heading psi (rad)."""
        p = self.kp * error
        self.integral += error * dt
        i = self.ki * self.integral

        # --- derivative: Case 6 (on measurement) + Case 7 (low-pass) ---
        if self.prev_measurement is None:
            d_raw = 0.0                          # no history yet -> no kick
        else:
            d_raw = -(measurement - self.prev_measurement) / dt   # note the minus
        self.prev_measurement = measurement
        self.d_filt = self.alpha * self.d_filt + (1.0 - self.alpha) * d_raw
        d = self.kd * self.d_filt

        u = p + i + d
        return max(self.out_min, min(self.out_max, u))            # clamp
🛠️ Two subtleties worth a second look

First, the prev_measurement is None guard: on the very first sample after a reset() there's no previous heading, so we emit $d=0$ rather than differentiating against garbage — exactly the same guard the Chapter 01 skeleton used for prev_error. Second, the filter sits on the raw derivative, before $k_d$ — so changing $k_d$ rescales the damping without touching the filter's noise behaviour.

6The decision recipe

① Intuition when to reach for which knob

These two fixes are not optional polish — they are the difference between a D term that helps and one that hurts. The decision tree is short:

SymptomCauseFixCost
One-sample rudder spike whenever the setpoint changesDerivative kick — D differentiating the setpoint stepDifferentiate the measurement (sign-flipped), not the errorNone — identical when setpoint is steady
Rudder chatters / servo grinds while holding a steady headingD amplifying sensor noise (high-pass behaviour)Low-pass the derivative, $\alpha\approx 0.85$~0.28 s lag on D → slightly weaker damping
Still too noisy even after filteringThe measurement itself is badFuse gyro + compass upstream (complementary / EKF)More code, but the right answer
∑ Both fixes share one idea

Each fix is about what you let the derivative see. The kick fix removes the setpoint from D's input; the noise fix removes high-frequency content from D's input. Same philosophy, two frequency bands: don't differentiate things that aren't the boat's real motion.

7Your turn

Q1 Conceptual check
Why does differentiating the measurement instead of the error eliminate the derivative kick without changing the controller's normal behaviour?
  • Because the measurement is always smaller than the error, so its derivative is smaller too.
  • When the setpoint is constant, $de/dt = -d\psi/dt$, so the two are identical — but the measurement contains no setpoint step, so a setpoint jump can't enter D.
  • Because the low-pass filter removes the spike anyway, so the choice of signal doesn't matter.
  • Because the measurement updates more slowly than the setpoint.
From the identity $\frac{de}{dt} = -\frac{d\psi}{dt} + \frac{\Delta\psi_\text{set}}{dt}$, the only difference between the two formulations is the $\Delta\psi_\text{set}/dt$ term. Hold the setpoint still and it's zero — the two signals match exactly, so you lose nothing. Step the setpoint and that term is the kick — and it lives entirely in the error, never in the measurement. Differentiating $\psi$ (with the sign flip) keeps the damping and drops the kick.
Q2 Compute it
With D-on-error and $k_d = 3$, the setpoint steps 10° in one $dt = 0.05$ s sample. What is the magnitude of the D contribution, expressed in degrees of commanded rudder? (Hint: $\dot e = 10°/0.05$ s, convert to rad/s, multiply by $k_d$, convert back to degrees.)
° rudder
$\dot e = 10°/0.05\text{ s} = 200\,°/\text{s} = 3.49\,\text{rad/s}$. Then $D = k_d\,\dot e = 3 \times 3.49 = 10.47\,\text{rad} = 600°$. On a ±35° rudder this saturates instantly for one sample — a kick that beats up the servo and does nothing useful. That's exactly the spike you toggle away in the sim.
Q3 Derivation — the filter's lag
You're tempted to push the derivative filter to $\alpha = 0.95$ to crush the noise further. Estimate the effective lag that adds to D, and explain in control terms why that's risky.
Hint
The effective time constant of an EMA is $\tau_\text{filt} = \dfrac{\alpha}{1-\alpha}\,dt$. Compare $\alpha = 0.85$ to $\alpha = 0.95$ at $dt = 0.05$ s, then think about what extra delay in the damping term does to a loop.
Show one solution

At $\alpha = 0.85$: $\tau_\text{filt} = \frac{0.85}{0.15}\times 0.05 \approx 0.28$ s. At $\alpha = 0.95$: $\tau_\text{filt} = \frac{0.95}{0.05}\times 0.05 = 0.95$ s — more than triple the lag, for only a modest extra noise reduction (output noise drops from $\approx 0.285\sigma_d$ to $\approx 0.16\sigma_d$).

Why that's risky: the D term exists to anticipate the boat's swing and oppose it early. Delaying D by ~1 s means it opposes the swing the boat had a second ago, not the one happening now — late damping is weak damping, and the overshoot D was killing creeps back. Worse, added phase lag is the universal route to instability at high gain (Chapter 09). The right move when 0.85 isn't enough is to fix the measurement — fuse gyro + compass — not to keep cranking $\alpha$ and starving your damping of timeliness.

🔑 Chapter recap

The error changes for two reasons — the boat moved or you moved the setpoint — and plain $de/dt$ reacts to both. A setpoint step gives $\dot e = 200\,°/\text{s} \Rightarrow \approx 600°$ of rudder for one sample: the derivative kick. Fix it by differentiating the measurement (sign-flipped); it's identical when the setpoint is steady and blind to setpoint jumps. Separately, D is a high-pass, so a $\sigma=0.5°$ compass yields $\approx 42°$ of rudder hash that grinds the servo while the boat holds course. Fix it with a low-pass on the derivative, $\alpha\approx0.85$, dropping chatter to ~28% — at the cost of ~0.28 s of lag and slightly weaker damping. The deeper fix for noise is upstream sensor fusion. Next: angle wrapping — the classic vehicle bug that turns the boat 340° the wrong way.
