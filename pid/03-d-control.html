D Control: The Damper

P alone overshoots and rings — it's a spring with no friction. In this chapter we keep $k_p=2$ fixed and add the derivative term. We'll watch D fight P on the way in, pin down the exact instant the controller decides to coast, and prove the one thing D can never do.

⏱ ~30 min🧭 PD heading hold⚡ Live sim🛑 Damping

1Why P needs a partner

① Intuition where we left off

Last chapter the P-only boat behaved like a mass on a spring. Command a turn from 0° to 90° and the rudder pulls harder the further you are from the setpoint — exactly like a spring pulls harder the more you stretch it. The trouble is what a lightly-damped spring does: it oscillates. The bow swings to 90°, but it arrives moving fast, sails straight through, and rings.

Concretely, with the gains we settled on, $k_p=2$ rings once and settles near 92°. Push to $k_p=5$ and it never stops — that's the ultimate gain $K_u$ of this boat, a $\pm16°$ oscillation forever. The fix is not more spring. It's friction. That's the derivative term.

💡 The one-sentence model of D

P reacts to where you are (the error). D reacts to how fast that's changing (the error's rate). A shock absorber doesn't care where the car is — only how fast the suspension is moving. D is the shock absorber.

② Math the term we're adding

We keep the same discrete control law from Chapter 01 and simply switch on $k_d$. The derivative is a finite difference of the (already wrapped) error:

$$ u_n = \underbrace{k_p\,e_n}_{\text{P: position}} \;+\; \underbrace{k_d\,\frac{e_n - e_{n-1}}{dt}}_{\text{D: velocity}}, \qquad e_n = \psi_\text{set} - \psi_n $$

Read the sign carefully. When the boat is swinging toward the setpoint, the error is shrinking, so $e_n < e_{n-1}$ and the finite difference is negative. With $k_d > 0$, D therefore comes out negative — it pushes the rudder the opposite way to P. D opposes P on the approach. That opposition is the whole mechanism.

2Drive it: add the damper yourself

④ Real case feel it before you trust the math

Same boat as always: $K=0.6$, $T=2.5$ s, 20 Hz loop, $\pm35°$ rudder. The P gain is pinned at $k_p=2$. The only knob that matters here is $k_d$. Run a 0° → 90° step and watch the heading panel.

🧭 PD heading hold (add the damper kd)
🔑 What to drag, what to watch

Sweep $k_d$ and reproduce the three cases from the notes:

  • $k_d=0$ — pure P. It overshoots to roughly 118° and rings. This is your baseline ugliness.
  • $k_d=1$ — one small, polite overshoot. The damper is biting.
  • $k_d=3$ — it glides in like a parking valet. No overshoot at all.

Now watch the rudder panel as the bow nears 90°: with $k_d=3$ the command actually goes negative — counter-rudder — well before the boat arrives. That negative blip is D braking. P would never command counter-rudder while still short of the target; only D does.

🛠️ Push it too far

Crank $k_d$ toward 8–10. The approach gets sluggish and the rudder starts to look twitchy. Too much damper makes the boat afraid of its own motion — and (foreshadowing Ch 07) it amplifies sensor noise into rudder chatter. There's a sweet spot, and $k_d=3$ is it for this hull.

3D opposes P: the worked example, re-read

② Math the cancellation in numbers

The cleanest proof that D fights P lives in the worked timestep from Chapter 01. Recall the mid-maneuver snapshot — heading 60°, setpoint 90°, previous error 31°, gains $k_p=2,\ k_d=3$:

∑ Read that again

The boat is still 30° short of the setpoint, and yet P (+60° of rudder) and D (−60°) exactly cancel. The controller commands essentially zero rudder. Why? Because the bow is already swinging in at 20 °/s. Pushing harder wouldn't get there sooner — it would only build more momentum to overshoot with. That cancellation is damping. Compare the P-only world: P alone would have demanded +60°, clamped to the +35° stop, full speed into the wall.

This is why the PD boat in the sim eases off long before it arrives. D is reading the closing rate and quietly subtracting it from the demand. The faster you're closing, the more it subtracts.

4The coasting rule: when does it go neutral?

② Math derive the threshold

The cancellation above wasn't luck. The controller goes neutral — P + D = 0 — exactly when the two terms are equal and opposite. Set them against each other:

$$ k_p\,e = k_d\,\dot e \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \dot e = \frac{k_p}{k_d}\,e $$

(I'm writing $\dot e$ as a positive closing rate here; on the approach $\dot e$ is actually negative and it's the magnitudes that balance.) Plug in our gains, $k_p=2$ and $k_d=3$:

$$ \dot e = \frac{k_p}{k_d}\,e = \frac{2}{3}\,e $$
🔑 The rule of thumb

At $k_p=2,\ k_d=3$, the controller stops pushing once the closing rate (°/s) exceeds ⅔ of the remaining error (°). So at 30° out, the threshold is $\tfrac{2}{3}\times 30 = 20$ °/s — close faster than that and D wins, the rudder eases off. That's precisely the worked example: 30° out, closing at 20 °/s, exactly on the knife-edge where P and D cancel.

The ratio $k_p/k_d$ is the controller's idea of a safe approach speed per degree remaining. Lower it (more $k_d$, or less $k_p$) and the boat insists on creeping in slowly — heavily damped. Raise it (less $k_d$) and it's willing to arrive fast — under-damped, overshoots. Tuning D is choosing how aggressively the boat is allowed to coast into its target.

5D acts on change only — and what that costs

① Intuition D's blind spot

Here is the single most important limitation, and it falls straight out of the math. D is proportional to $\dot e$, the rate of change of error. The moment the boat settles onto a heading and holds it, the error stops changing: $\dot e = 0$, so D = 0. The damper disappears the instant the motion stops. That's exactly what you want from a damper — but it means D contributes nothing to holding a steady heading.

⚠️ D cannot fix offset, and it cannot do the turn

Recall Case 2: the current pushes the bow like 4° of stuck rudder, and a P controller settles with a standing offset because at zero error it commands zero rudder. Adding D changes none of this. When the boat sits at its (wrong) steady heading, the error is constant, $\dot e=0$, and D = 0 — it has nothing to grab. D only tames motion; it cannot supply the constant rudder a steady disturbance demands. Killing that offset is a different term's job entirely (the integral — next chapter).

② Math two boundary conditions

Both failures of D are the same fact seen twice. At a steady offset heading the error is some constant $e_\text{ss} \neq 0$:

$$ \dot e = 0 \;\Rightarrow\; \text{D} = k_d\,\dot e = 0, \qquad u_\text{ss} = k_p\,e_\text{ss} + 0 = k_p\,e_\text{ss} $$

The steady-state rudder is set by P alone — D added nothing. So PD lands on the same offset as P-only against a current. D makes the journey smooth; it does not change the destination. Hold that thought: it's the cliffhanger for the I term.

6The D term in code

③ Code read every line

This is the same PID class from Chapter 01 — we're just looking hard at the two lines that are the derivative. Note the guard on the very first sample: with no prev_error yet, the finite difference is undefined, so D is forced to zero. Skip that guard and your first timestep computes a garbage derivative against None and slams the rudder.

pid_controller.py Python
def update(self, error, dt):
    """One PD step. error is already angle-wrapped (rad)."""
    p = self.kp * error

    # --- the derivative term ---------------------------------
    # First sample: no previous error, so the rate is undefined.
    # Force D = 0 rather than differencing against None.
    if self.prev_error is None:
        d = 0.0
    else:
        de = error - self.prev_error          # change in error this step
        d  = self.kd * de / dt                # rad rudder; negative while closing in
    self.prev_error = error                    # remember for next call
    # ---------------------------------------------------------

    u = p + d                                  # (ki*integral added in Ch 04)
    return max(self.out_min, min(self.out_max, u))   # clamp to rudder stops
④ Real case verify the worked example

Drive the same numbers through it as a sanity check — this should reproduce the hand calculation exactly. Notice we wrap every error before it reaches the controller (that angle-wrap bug is its own chapter), and we pass the real loop period as dt.

check_worked_example.py Python
import math

pid = PID(kp=2.0, ki=0.0, kd=3.0, output_limits=(-0.611, 0.611))  # ±35°
DT  = 0.05

# Seed the controller with the "previous" sample: heading 59°, sp 90°
prev_heading = math.radians(59.0)
pid.update(math.radians(90.0) - prev_heading, DT)   # prev_error = 31° = 0.5411 rad

# Now the snapshot step: heading 60°, setpoint 90°
e = math.radians(90.0) - math.radians(60.0)         # 30° = 0.5236 rad
u = pid.update(e, DT)

print(f"P  = {pid.kp * e:+.3f} rad   ({math.degrees(pid.kp*e):+.1f}°)")
print(f"de = {e - math.radians(31.0):+.4f} rad   over dt = {DT}s")
print(f"u  = {u:+.3f} rad   ({math.degrees(u):+.1f}°)")
# P  = +1.047 rad   (+60.0°)
# de = -0.0175 rad  -> D = 3 * (-0.0175/0.05) = -1.047 rad (-60.0°)
# u  = +0.000 rad   (+0.0°)   <- P and D cancel: the boat coasts
⚓ On the real USV

On the water, $\dot e$ comes from differencing a noisy compass at 20 Hz — and the derivative of noise is enormous (two adjacent 0.5° readings can imply 14 °/s of phantom swing). That's why production code differentiates the measurement, not the error, and low-pass filters it. Both are decisions you make right here in update(); we'll meet the bugs that force them in Ch 06–07. For now, ideal sensor, clean D.

7Your turn

⑤ Practice make the rules yours
Q1 Conceptual check
The PD boat ($k_p=2,\ k_d=3$) holds a steady heading against the current but settles 2° off the setpoint — the same offset the P-only boat had. Why didn't adding D remove the offset?
  • $k_d=3$ is too small; a larger $k_d$ would close the gap.
  • At a steady heading the error is constant, so $\dot e = 0$ and D = 0 — D contributes nothing while holding, leaving P alone to fight the current.
  • D adds its own offset that happens to cancel out.
  • The current grew while the boat settled.
D is proportional to $\dot e$. Once the boat is steady, $\dot e = 0$, so $u_\text{ss} = k_p e_\text{ss} + k_d\cdot 0 = k_p e_\text{ss}$ — identical to P-only. D smooths the approach but cannot supply the constant rudder a steady disturbance demands. That's the integral's job (Ch 04). No amount of $k_d$ changes the destination.
Q2 Compute it
Using the coasting rule $\dot e = \tfrac{k_p}{k_d}\,e$ with $k_p=2,\ k_d=3$: the boat is 20° short of its setpoint. Above what closing rate (in °/s) does D start to beat P and the controller eases off the rudder?
°/s
$\dot e = \tfrac{2}{3}\times 20° = 13.33$ °/s. Below this, P still dominates and the rudder keeps pushing into the turn; above it, D dominates and the controller starts braking. Notice the threshold scales with how far out you are — at 30° it was 20 °/s, at 20° it's 13.3 °/s. The closer you get, the more cautious the controller becomes about your speed.
Q3 Reasoning — the negative rudder blip
In the sim with $k_d=3$, the rudder command goes briefly negative (counter-rudder) while the boat is still short of 90° and turning toward it. Explain why, and explain why a pure-P controller could never do this.
Hint
Write out $u = k_p e + k_d \dot e$ near the end of the approach. The error $e$ is still positive (you haven't arrived). What sign is $\dot e$, and how big is D relative to P when the bow is swinging in fast?
Show one solution

Near the end of the approach the error is small but positive ($e > 0$, still short) while the closing rate is large, so $\dot e$ is strongly negative. Then $\text{D} = k_d\dot e$ is a large negative number and $\text{P} = k_p e$ is a small positive one. Their sum $u = k_p e + k_d\dot e$ goes negative: the controller commands counter-rudder to brake the swing before the boat reaches the setpoint, killing the momentum that would otherwise cause overshoot.

A pure-P controller computes $u = k_p e$. While $e > 0$ (short of target), $u$ is always positive — it cannot command counter-rudder until the boat has already overshot and $e$ flips sign. By then the momentum is built and you ring. Anticipatory braking is uniquely D's contribution: it acts on the rate, so it can react before the position error tells it to.

🔑 Chapter recap

D is the damper: $\text{D}=k_d\,\dot e$. On the approach the error is shrinking, so $\dot e < 0$ and D opposes P, braking the swing before the boat arrives — that's the P+D cancellation in the worked example (30° out, 20 °/s in, P and D both ±60°, net zero). The coasting rule $k_p e = k_d\dot e$ gives the neutral threshold: at $k_p=2,\,k_d=3$, ease off once closing faster than ⅔ of the remaining error. But D acts on change only: once steady, $\dot e=0$ and D=0, so it cannot fix the standing offset against a current and cannot do the turn — it only tames motion. Holding the rudder while error is zero needs the integral. Next: I, the trim tab.
