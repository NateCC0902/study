Angle Wrapping & Real Actuators

Two bugs that never show up in the clean math but always show up on the water. First: a controller that does flawless PID and still turns 340° the long way around, because nobody told it that 350° and 10° are neighbours. Second: the reason "raise $k_p$ until it oscillates" actually works on a real boat — when our idealized model swore it never would.

⏱ ~25 min🧭 Angle wrapping⚡ Live sim🐌 Servo slew & phase lag

1The 340° bug: when subtraction lies

① Intuition picture the compass

The boat is heading 350° — just west of north. You hand it a new setpoint: 10° — just east of north. To a sailor that's trivial: nudge the bow 20° to starboard and you're done. To a naive controller it's a disaster, because the controller doesn't know the compass is a circle. It does the one thing it knows how to do — subtract.

💡 The whole bug in one line

$e = 10° - 350° = -340°$. The controller reads that as "turn 340° clockwise" and commits to the scenic route — all the way around the compass — to reach a heading that was 20° away.

This is Case 8 from the notes, and it is the single most famous bug in vehicle control. It hides perfectly: every test you run that doesn't straddle north passes clean. Then on the day a waypoint sits just past the 360°/0° seam, the boat spins like a confused dog chasing its tail. On open water it's comedy; ten metres from a dock it's a hull repair.

Notice the error doesn't enter the boat anywhere yet — this is purely the arithmetic the controller does before the first P, I, or D term. Get this wrong and a perfectly tuned PID faithfully executes a catastrophic command. The gains are innocent; the error definition is the bug.

2Wrapping to (−π, π]: the math that fixes it

② Math one modulo, done right

Heading lives on a circle, so an error of $-340°$ and an error of $+20°$ point the bow at exactly the same place — they differ by a full turn, $360°$. The fix is to take whichever representative of the angle lands in the half-open interval $(-\pi, \pi]$. That interval is the set of "shortest signed turns": at most a half-circle either way.

$$ \mathrm{wrap}(\theta) \;=\; \big((\theta + \pi)\bmod 2\pi\big) - \pi \;\in\; (-\pi,\,\pi] $$

Walk it through with our numbers. The raw error is $\theta = -340° = -5.934$ rad. Add $\pi$, take the modulo by $2\pi$, subtract $\pi$:

StepComputationResult
raw error$10° - 350°$$-340° = -5.9341$ rad
shift by $\pi$$-5.9341 + 3.1416$$-2.7925$ rad
$\bmod\,2\pi$$-2.7925 \bmod 6.2832$$3.4907$ rad
shift back$3.4907 - 3.1416$$0.3491$ rad $= +20°$
∑ Why the $+\pi \ldots -\pi$ sandwich

A bare $\theta \bmod 2\pi$ maps everything into $[0, 2\pi)$ — so $-340°$ becomes $+20°$ ✅, but $+190°$ stays $+190°$ instead of collapsing to $-170°$ ❌, and you've fixed half the cases. Shifting by $\pi$ first centres the wrap window on zero, so both over- and under-shoots fold to the nearest equivalent. One caveat: the language's modulo must return a non-negative result for negative inputs. Python's % does; C's fmod and JavaScript's % do not — for those, add $2\pi$ and take the modulo again, or use atan2(sin θ, cos θ), which is branch-free and handles the sign for you.

That last trick is worth memorising. Since $\sin$ and $\cos$ are periodic, $\operatorname{atan2}(\sin\theta, \cos\theta)$ returns the canonical angle in $(-\pi, \pi]$ regardless of how many turns $\theta$ has accumulated, with no modulo-sign surprises:

$$ \mathrm{wrap}(\theta) \;=\; \operatorname{atan2}\!\big(\sin\theta,\; \cos\theta\big) $$

3See it spin: the live boat

④ Real case watch the bug, then kill it

Here is the exact scenario from Case 8: the boat starts at 350° heading, the setpoint is 10°, and you can toggle the angle-wrap fix on and off. With the fix off you're watching a naive controller; with it on, the same gains do the sane thing.

🧭 Start at 350° heading for 10° — toggle the angle-wrap fix
🔑 What to drag, what to watch

Toggle the wrap fix off. The error reads $-340°$, the rudder slams to its $-35°$ stop, and the bow swings away from 10° — it grinds the entire long way around the compass to arrive at a heading it was 20° from. The heading trace climbs (or plunges) by hundreds of degrees. Now toggle it on. The error snaps to $+20°$, the rudder takes a gentle starboard bite, and the boat settles on 10° in seconds. Same plant, same $k_p,k_i,k_d$ — the only difference is one line of arithmetic on the error.

Push it further: nudge the setpoint a few degrees either side of north and watch the error never exceed $\pm 180°$ once wrapping is on. That bounded error is the whole point — the controller now always knows the shortest way home.

4The fix in code (and where it goes)

③ Code one function, every cycle

The wrap function is four characters of logic and belongs on every error before it touches the PID — not just at setpoint changes. Heading drifts continuously, so the seam can be crossed at any sample, not only when you command a new waypoint.

wrap.py Python
import math

def wrap(angle):
    """Fold any angle into (-pi, pi] — the shortest signed turn."""
    return (angle + math.pi) % (2 * math.pi) - math.pi

# --- inside the control loop, EVERY cycle ---
error = wrap(setpoint - heading)        # rad, never larger than +/- pi
u     = pid.update(error, dt)           # the PID never sees a raw angle

Demonstrating the bug and the fix side by side makes the failure visceral. Both controllers below are identical except one wraps:

demo_wrap.py Python
heading, setpoint = math.radians(350), math.radians(10)

naive  = setpoint - heading                 # -5.934 rad  = -340 deg
fixed  = wrap(setpoint - heading)           #  0.349 rad  =  +20 deg

print(f"naive error: {math.degrees(naive):+.0f} deg")   # naive error: -340 deg
print(f"wrapped err: {math.degrees(fixed):+.0f} deg")   # wrapped err:  +20 deg

# The naive boat commands kp * (-5.934) of rudder -> pinned at the -35 deg stop,
# and turns 340 deg the WRONG way. The wrapped boat asks for a 20-deg nudge.
⚠️ The places people forget to wrap

It's not enough to wrap once. Wrap the error feeding P and I; if you do derivative-on-error you must wrap that difference too (Ch 06 made you switch to derivative-on-measurement, which sidesteps it). And in ROS 2, your yaw from /odometry/filtered is already in $(-\pi, \pi]$ from the quaternion — but the moment you do any math on it, re-wrap. The bug is invisible until your test crosses north.

5Why high $k_p$ oscillates on a real boat

① Intuition an honest confession

Now Case 9, and it starts with an embarrassing admission about our model. A bare first-order Nomoto boat with an ideal rudder never goes unstable from $k_p$ alone. In early tests, even $k_p = 100$ settled fine. So if you've been told all course "crank $k_p$ until it rings, then back off" — on this idealized plant, that recipe shouldn't work at all. Yet it works on every real boat. What gives?

💡 The one-word answer

Lag. Real actuators can't move instantly. Our rudder servo slews at $25°/\text{s}$ — it physically takes time to swing. At high $k_p$ the controller demands rail-to-rail flips faster than the servo can follow, so the rudder is always executing what the boat needed a second ago. That stale correction is phase lag, and phase lag plus high gain is the universal recipe for oscillation.

② Math slew rate becomes delay

The servo's stop-to-stop travel is the full rudder range, $\pm 35° = 70°$. At a slew rate of $25°/\text{s}$ it cannot do that move faster than

$$ t_{\text{stop-to-stop}} \;=\; \frac{70°}{25°/\text{s}} \;=\; 2.8\ \text{s} $$

So when a hot P controller commands a square wave — slam to $+35°$, then $-35°$, then back — the actual rudder traces a sawtooth chasing it, never arriving before the command flips again. The rudder angle the boat actually feels is delayed relative to the command. Approximate the slew limiter as a pure transport delay $\tau_d$; for fast oscillation it adds phase $-\omega\tau_d$ to the loop. When the accumulated phase from the hull lag $T$ plus this delay reaches $-180°$ at a frequency where the gain still exceeds 1, the loop sustains oscillation. More $k_p$ raises that crossover frequency, where the delay's phase penalty $-\omega\tau_d$ is larger — so past some gain it always tips over.

∑ Why the idealized model fooled us

A single pole (the hull's $T = 2.5$ s lag) contributes at most $-90°$ of phase — it can never reach the $-180°$ that a pure-gain loop needs to oscillate. That's why ideal $k_p = 100$ settled. Add the servo's transport-delay phase $-\omega\tau_d$, which grows without bound as frequency rises, and you finally get to $-180°$. Reality always brings extra lag: sensor filtering, comms latency, thruster spin-up, hull hydrodynamics. More lag → lower usable $k_p$.

④ Real case the sawtooth that eats servos

This is exactly Case 9's plot: command (thin) is a square wave; the actual rudder (thick) is a lagging sawtooth. The boat oscillates not because the math is unstable, but because the actuator is always a beat behind. And it's why our sim has the $25°/\text{s}$ slew limit baked in — tuning against an unrealistically perfect plant teaches you gains that fall apart the instant you hit the water.

🛠️ The practical takeaway for tuning

The "raise $k_p$ until sustained oscillation" recipe (the ultimate-gain method) works precisely because your real actuator supplies the missing phase lag. On this boat that ultimate gain is $K_u \approx 5$ with period $T_u \approx 9$ s; back off to half, $k_p \approx 2$–$2.5$. Tune against the real plant — slew limit and all — never the textbook ideal.

6Two bugs, one lesson

These cases look unrelated — one is arithmetic, one is hardware — but they teach the same thing: the clean model is a lie of omission, and the omissions bite.

  1. Angle wrapping is the model forgetting that heading is circular. Fix: $\mathrm{wrap}(\theta) \in (-\pi,\pi]$ on every error, every cycle.
  2. Actuator slew is the model forgetting that the rudder is physical. It adds phase lag, which is what makes high $k_p$ oscillate — and what makes the tuning recipe valid.
⚠️ The meta-bug

Both failures are invisible in clean testing. The wrap bug hides until you cross north; the slew bug hides until you tune against a real servo. The discipline is to put reality into your tests — cross the seam on purpose, and tune the plant you'll actually deploy on.

Q1 Conceptual check
A boat at 350° is told to hold 10°. With no angle wrapping, what does the naive controller do, and why?
  • Settles correctly on 10° — subtraction always gives the right turn.
  • Computes $e=-340°$, so it turns nearly all the way around the compass the long way to reach a heading 20° away.
  • Blows up because the Nomoto model is unstable at any setpoint near north.
  • Stalls — the error is so large the integral windup cancels the P term.
Raw subtraction gives $10°-350°=-340°$. The controller has no idea heading is circular, so it commands a huge negative correction — full rudder the wrong way — and grinds 340° around to reach a point that was a 20° nudge to starboard. Wrapping to $(-\pi,\pi]$ turns that $-340°$ into $+20°$.
Q2 Compute it
The rudder travels $\pm 35°$ (so $70°$ stop-to-stop) and slews at $25°/\text{s}$. How long, in seconds, does it take the servo to swing from one stop to the other? (This delay is the phase lag that makes high $k_p$ oscillate.)
s
$t = \dfrac{70°}{25°/\text{s}} = 2.8$ s. When a hot P controller flips the command rail-to-rail faster than that, the actual rudder traces a lagging sawtooth — always doing what the boat needed almost three seconds ago. That transport delay is the missing phase that an ideal single-pole plant never had, and it's why "crank $k_p$ until it rings" works on real hardware.
Q3 Derivation — wrap 200° by hand
Using $\mathrm{wrap}(\theta) = \big((\theta+\pi)\bmod 2\pi\big)-\pi$, wrap an error of $+200°$. Show each step, give the answer in degrees, and explain why the naive $\theta \bmod 2\pi$ would get this case wrong.
Hint
Work in degrees with $\pi \to 180°$ and $2\pi \to 360°$: add $180$, take mod $360$ (result must be non-negative), subtract $180$. Then ask: what does $200° \bmod 360°$ give, and is that the shortest turn?
Show one solution

Step by step in degrees: shift, $200 + 180 = 380$; modulo, $380 \bmod 360 = 20$; shift back, $20 - 180 = \mathbf{-160°}$. So a $+200°$ error wraps to $\mathbf{-160°}$ — turn $160°$ the other way, which is shorter than $200°$.

The naive $\theta \bmod 2\pi$ would give $200° \bmod 360° = 200°$ — unchanged, and not the shortest turn (the boat would swing $200°$ when $160°$ the other way reaches the same heading). The $+\pi\ldots-\pi$ sandwich centres the wrap window on zero so over-shoots like this fold to the near side. That's the whole reason for the two extra shifts.

🔑 Chapter recap

Heading is circular, so define error as $\mathrm{wrap}(\text{setpoint}-\psi) \in (-\pi,\pi]$ — every cycle, before P/I/D, or a perfect PID will turn 340° the wrong way (Case 8). And the reason "raise $k_p$ until it oscillates" works isn't in the ideal model — a single hull pole can't reach $-180°$ phase. It's the real actuator: the $25°/\text{s}$ servo needs $2.8$ s stop-to-stop, and that delay is the phase lag that destabilizes high gain (Case 9). Both bugs hide in clean tests; put reality into your tests and tune the plant you'll deploy. That closes the loop on a controller that survives the water — next, the full tuning walkthrough.
