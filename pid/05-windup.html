PID Course · Chapter 05 · The integral's dark side

Integral Windup & Anti-Windup

In Chapter 04 the integral was the hero — it learned the current and trimmed the boat to zero error. Now we hand it a big maneuver, watch the rudder slam into its stop, and discover the integral has no idea the rudder is maxed. It keeps accumulating. Then it owns the boat. We'll watch it happen, do the arithmetic, and bolt on three different fixes.

⏱ ~30 min🧨 750° of stored rudder⚡ Live sim🛡️ 3 anti-windup fixes

1The setup: a turn big enough to saturate

① Intuition where the trouble starts

Everything so far has been a polite maneuver: a 90° turn, gentle gains, the rudder never near its limit. Now we make it ugly. Command a 180° turn — point the boat at a waypoint directly behind it — with a hot integral: $k_p=2,\ k_d=3,\ k_i=1$. That $k_i$ is five times the value we settled on in Chapter 04, and it's about to matter enormously.

The error at the start of a 180° turn is $\pi$ radians. Multiply by $k_p=2$ and the P term alone wants $2\pi \approx 360°$ of rudder. The rudder stop is $\pm35°$. So the command is pinned hard at $+35°$ from the very first sample, and it stays pinned for the whole sweep — about 9 seconds while the boat lumbers around. During those 9 seconds the controller is, in a real sense, no longer in control: the rudder is doing the maximum it can do, and nothing the math computes can change that.

💡 The one-sentence bug

While the rudder is saturated, the integral keeps adding up the error as if its output still mattered — but it doesn't, because the actuator is already maxed. The integral is filling a bucket whose tap is already wide open.

④ Real case this is not exotic

This is the single most common bug every hobby-autopilot author meets. Command a USV to a waypoint behind it, the rudder saturates, the integral winds up, and the boat does something insane. It happens any time demand exceeds what the actuator can deliver: a drone commanded to climb faster than full throttle allows, a thermostat with the heater already flat-out, a motor at its current limit. Saturation plus an integrator is a universal trap.

2Watch it happen

① Intuition two boats, one turn

Here is the exact experiment. The same 180° turn, the same hot gains, run two ways: with the integral protected (anti-windup ON) and with it left naked (OFF). Toggle the switch and compare. Watch the heading panel and the rudder panel together — the rudder panel is where the crime is committed; the heading panel is where you find the body.

🧭 Hard 180° turn — toggle anti-windup on/off
🔑 What to drag, what to watch

Flip anti-windup ON: the boat makes a clean 180° turn, settles, done — the protected integral never bloats. Flip it OFF and watch the boat blow straight through 180°. By the time the error finally changes sign, the integral is so engorged it keeps the rudder pinned the wrong way — the boat does donuts forever, heading climbing past 2000°, the integral half-unwinding each lap and re-winding, never recovering. Same plant, same gains. The only difference is whether the integral is allowed to lie to itself while the rudder is maxed.

⚠️ The integral outlives the maneuver

The fatal detail: windup doesn't hurt you during the turn — the rudder is maxed either way, so both boats turn identically at first. It hurts you after. The bloated integral is a debt that has to be paid back error-by-error, and there isn't enough negative error in a single overshoot to pay it. So the boat never stops.

3The arithmetic: how 750° of rudder gets stored

② Math count the accumulation

Let's make the disaster quantitative, because the number is genuinely shocking. Recall the discrete integral term: every sample, we add $e\cdot dt$ to a running accumulator, then multiply by $k_i$ to get the I contribution.

$$ \text{integral} \;\mathrel{+}=\; e \cdot dt, \qquad I = k_i \cdot \text{integral} $$

During the 180° turn the error sweeps from $\pi \approx 3.14$ rad down toward $0$, so its average over the maneuver is roughly $1.5$ rad. The turn lasts about $9$ s. So the accumulator grows by:

$$ \Delta\,\text{integral} \;\approx\; \bar e \cdot \Delta t \;=\; 1.5\ \text{rad} \times 9\ \text{s} \;=\; 13.5\ \text{rad·s} $$

With $k_i = 1$, the I term alone is then $k_i \cdot \text{integral} \approx 13$ rad. Convert to degrees:

$$ I \;\approx\; 13\ \text{rad} \times \frac{180°}{\pi} \;\approx\; \mathbf{750°}\ \text{of commanded rudder} $$
∑ Read that number

The rudder stop is $\pm35°$. The integral term alone is demanding 750° — more than twenty times the physical limit, and that's before we add P and D. To unwind 750° of stored rudder, the controller needs the error to be negative for a long time. One overshoot past 180° doesn't supply nearly enough negative-error-seconds. So the integral stays saturated, the rudder stays pinned, and the boat keeps turning. Donuts.

① Intuition the bucket analogy, finished

Think of the accumulator as a bucket the integral keeps pouring into. Normally the I output drains the bucket: when error reaches zero the integral stops growing, and any sign-flip in error drains it back down. But while the rudder is saturated, the I output is capped — the drain is choked — yet we keep pouring. By the time the maneuver ends, the bucket is overflowing with 750° of water that has to be bailed out one teaspoon (one negative-error sample) at a time.

4Why clamping the output isn't enough

② Math the subtle trap

"But we already clamp the output," you object — and you're right; Chapter 01's update() ends with u = clamp(u, -RUDDER_MAX, +RUDDER_MAX). That clamp keeps the command we send the servo sane. It does nothing for the integral, which lives upstream of the clamp and keeps growing regardless. The clamp throws away the excess command silently — and that discarded excess is exactly the windup.

The lesson: the integral must be told, somehow, that the actuator is saturated, so it stops accumulating debt it can't act on. There are three standard ways to tell it. They differ in elegance and smoothness, but all three break the feedback path between "huge error" and "ever-growing integral" while the rudder is pinned.

5Three fixes, from blunt to elegant

③ Code read every line

Fix 1 — Clamp the integral itself. The bluntest fix and the one the blue trace in the sim uses: cap the accumulator so that $k_i\cdot\text{integral}$ can never, by itself, exceed the rudder limit. Simple, robust, and impossible to get catastrophically wrong.

fix 1 · integral clamp Python
self.integral += error * dt
# cap so ki*integral stays within the actuator range
i_limit = self.out_max / max(self.ki, 1e-9)        # rad·s
self.integral = max(-i_limit, min(i_limit, self.integral))
i = self.ki * self.integral

Fix 2 — Conditional integration. Don't integrate on samples where the output is saturated. If the rudder is already pinned, accumulating more error is pointless, so skip the += on those steps. (A refinement: still allow integration in the direction that would reduce saturation, so the integral can start unwinding the instant the error helps.)

fix 2 · conditional integration Python
u_raw = self.kp*error + self.ki*self.integral + d   # before clamp
saturated = (u_raw > self.out_max) or (u_raw < self.out_min)

# only accumulate if NOT saturated, OR if this error pushes us back in-range
pushing_into_limit = (u_raw > self.out_max and error > 0) or \
                     (u_raw < self.out_min and error < 0)
if not (saturated and pushing_into_limit):
    self.integral += error * dt
i = self.ki * self.integral

Fix 3 — Back-calculation. The industry standard, and the smoothest. Each step, compute the unclamped command and the clamped command; feed the difference back into the integral as a corrective term scaled by a back-calc gain $k_b$. The more we're clamping, the harder we bleed the integral down. There's no hard switch, so no chattering at the saturation boundary.

$$ \text{integral} \;\mathrel{+}=\; e\cdot dt \;-\; k_b\,\big(u_\text{unclamped} - u_\text{clamped}\big)\,dt $$
fix 3 · back-calculation (recommended) Python
def update(self, error, dt):
    p = self.kp * error
    d = 0.0 if self.prev_error is None else \
        self.kd * (error - self.prev_error) / dt
    self.prev_error = error

    self.integral += error * dt
    i = self.ki * self.integral

    u_unclamped = p + i + d
    u = max(self.out_min, min(self.out_max, u_unclamped))   # clamp

    # back-calc: bleed the integral toward what the actuator can actually do
    self.integral -= self.kb * (u_unclamped - u) * dt       # kb ~ 1/ki
    return u
🛠️ Which one should you ship?

Start with Fix 1 — it's three lines and it can never blow up. Move to Fix 3 when you want smooth recovery and have a few minutes to tune $k_b$ (a good starting point is $k_b \approx 1/k_i$). Fix 2 is fine but the hard on/off switch can chatter right at the saturation edge; back-calculation is the same idea made continuous, which is why it's the textbook default.

6The cost of recovery: counting negative-error-seconds

② Math why one overshoot can't pay the debt

Let's prove to ourselves why the unprotected boat can't recover, because it sharpens the intuition for every saturating loop you'll ever build. The integral holds 13.5 rad·s of accumulated error. To drive it back to the modest value that just trims the current (about $-4°$ of rudder, i.e. $\text{integral} \approx -0.07$ rad·s with $k_i=1$), the error must integrate negatively by roughly that whole 13.5 rad·s.

A single overshoot past 180° might reach, say, 30° past — that's about $0.5$ rad of negative error, lasting maybe $4$ s before the boat is dragged back around. That contributes only:

$$ \int e\,dt \;\approx\; -0.5\ \text{rad} \times 4\ \text{s} \;=\; -2\ \text{rad·s} $$

Two rad·s paid against a thirteen-and-a-half rad·s debt. The integral is still hugely positive, the rudder is still pinned, the boat keeps turning — straight into another lap, where it re-accumulates what little it just shed. That's the mathematical signature of the donut: a debt that re-grows faster than any single overshoot can repay it.

🔑 The general rule

An integrator that has wound up by $W$ rad·s of error needs $W$ rad·s of opposite-sign error to unwind. If your maneuver can't naturally supply that much opposite error before the next saturation event, you will not recover without anti-windup. This is not specific to boats — it's arithmetic.

7On the real boat (ROS 2)

④ Real case where this bites you in the field

On an actual USV node, three things conspire to make windup worse than the sim suggests, so anti-windup isn't optional — it's a safety item:

  • Software limits are tighter than physical ones. You clamp the rudder to $\pm35°$ in software for mechanical safety, which means you saturate earlier and wind up more than the raw actuator would.
  • Re-engagement. When the operator flips from manual back to auto, a stale integral from minutes ago is itself a wound-up integrator. Always reset() the controller on re-engage — that's the windup bug the field-tuning notes warned you about.
  • Differential thrust. If you steer with left = base + u, right = base − u, the command saturates when base ± u hits the ESC limit. Same windup, just hidden one layer down in the mixer. Clamp and anti-windup at the PID, before the mixer.
🛰️ Field rule

Before any autonomy test: hardware kill switch armed, software rudder clamp in place, anti-windup enabled, and reset() wired to your auto-engage transition. A wound-up integral on the water doesn't just look silly — it ignores your stick inputs until it bleeds down, which near a dock is a collision.

8Your turn

Q1 Conceptual check
In the unprotected 180° turn, the boat does donuts forever. What is the direct cause?
  • The P term is too large and saturates the rudder.
  • The plant (Nomoto model) is unstable for any large turn.
  • The integral wound up far past the rudder limit during saturation, and one overshoot can't supply enough negative error to unwind it.
  • The derivative kick on the 180° setpoint step keeps re-saturating the rudder.
P saturating is normal and harmless on its own — the protected boat saturates P identically and still turns cleanly. The killer is the integral: ~13.5 rad·s (~750° of rudder) accumulated while the rudder was pinned, and a single overshoot only repays a couple of rad·s. The debt re-grows each lap, so the rudder stays pinned and the boat never stops.
Q2 Compute it
During the 9 s turn, average error $\bar e \approx 1.5$ rad and $k_i = 1$. The accumulator grows by $\bar e\cdot\Delta t = 13.5$ rad·s. What is the I term, $k_i\cdot\text{integral}$, expressed in degrees of commanded rudder?
° rudder
$k_i\cdot\text{integral} = 1 \times 13.5 = 13.5$ rad. Convert: $13.5 \times 180/\pi \approx 773°$ (we round it to "~750°" in the prose). Against a $\pm35°$ rudder stop, that's over 20× the physical limit demanded by the integral alone, before P and D are even added.
Q3 Derivation — why back-calculation self-limits
In back-calculation we add $-k_b(u_\text{unclamped}-u_\text{clamped})\,dt$ to the integral each step. Show why this drives the integral to a steady value where the unclamped command sits right at the saturation limit, and why that's exactly what we want.
Hint
A quantity stops changing when its net increment per step is zero. Write the total change to integral per step as the error term plus the back-calc term, and set it to zero.
Show one solution

Per step the integral changes by $\Delta = \big(e - k_b(u_\text{unclamped}-u_\text{clamped})\big)\,dt$. While saturated, $u_\text{clamped}=u_\text{max}$ is fixed and $u_\text{unclamped}$ rises with the integral. The back-calc term grows with the overshoot $(u_\text{unclamped}-u_\text{max})$, so it fights the $e\,dt$ growth. Setting $\Delta = 0$ gives the equilibrium $u_\text{unclamped}-u_\text{max} = e/k_b$: the integral settles so the unclamped command hovers just barely above the limit by a small amount proportional to the current error. The instant the error shrinks, that gap closes and the integral can move freely again — no hard switch, no chatter, and the integral is never more wound up than it needs to be to keep the rudder at the stop. That graceful, continuous self-limiting is why back-calculation beats both the hard integral clamp and the on/off conditional-integration switch.

🔑 Chapter recap

A maneuver big enough to saturate the rudder ($180°$ turn, hot $k_i=1$) lets the integral accumulate ~13.5 rad·s — about 750° of stored rudder — because the integrator can't see that the actuator is already maxed. Clamping the output doesn't help; the integral lives upstream of the clamp. After the turn, one overshoot can't supply enough negative-error-seconds to unwind that debt, so the rudder stays pinned and the boat does donuts forever. Three fixes break the windup: clamp the integral (blunt, robust), conditional integration (skip accumulation while saturated), and back-calculation (bleed the integral by the clamped excess — smoothest, industry standard).
