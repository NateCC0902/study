Analog: ADC, DMA Streaming, DAC & Oversampling

On Arduino you called analogRead() and got a 10-bit number, one channel at a time, with the CPU babysitting every conversion. The STM32 ADC is the same idea on steroids: 12-bit, multi-channel, and able to fire conversions and dump them straight into RAM via DMA while the CPU runs your control loop. We'll build that machine from the silicon up — and squeeze 14 effective bits out of a 12-bit converter with oversampling.

1The same idea, scaled up

① Intuition build the picture first

An ADC (analog-to-digital converter) is a voltmeter that answers in integers. Feed it a voltage between 0 and a reference $V_{ref}$, and it hands back a whole number proportional to where that voltage sits in the range. That's exactly what Arduino's analogRead() did. The STM32 changes three things, and each one matters:

💡 The one-sentence model

An STM32 ADC is a fast 12-bit voltmeter with a programmable schedule and a conveyor belt: it can convert a list of channels in sequence, on a timer's beat, and hand each result to DMA so the CPU never has to ask "are you done yet?"

The three upgrades over the classic AVR ADC:

  • Resolution. 12 bits instead of 10 — 4096 levels instead of 1024. Finer voltage steps.
  • Throughput. Millions of samples per second, not tens of thousands. A successive-approximation core clocked up to tens of MHz.
  • Autonomy. A whole sequence of channels can convert back-to-back, triggered by a timer, with results streamed to a RAM buffer by DMA. Zero CPU per sample.

That last point is the whole game. On Arduino, sampling 4 sensors at 10 kHz means the CPU is stuck in analogRead() 40,000 times a second. On STM32, you configure it once and the sensors fill a buffer behind your back. We'll get there in §4.

🔑 What you're actually building

A pipeline: analog pin → sample-and-hold → SAR converter → data register → DMA → RAM buffer. The CPU configures the front of the pipe and reads the back of it. Everything in between is hardware.

2Resolution: turning volts into counts

② Math derive it

A 12-bit converter divides the input range $[0, V_{ref}]$ into $2^{12} = 4096$ equal steps. The smallest voltage difference it can distinguish — one count — is called the LSB (least-significant bit):

$$ \text{LSB} = \frac{V_{ref}}{2^{n}} = \frac{V_{ref}}{4096} $$

With the typical $V_{ref} = 3.3\,\text{V}$:

$$ \text{LSB} = \frac{3.3\,\text{V}}{4096} \approx 0.806\,\text{mV} $$

To go the other way — from a raw ADC count back to a voltage — note that the code ranges from $0$ (for 0 V) to $4095$ (for full scale, just under $V_{ref}$). The converter reports the fraction of full scale, so:

$$ V = \frac{\text{adc}}{4095}\, V_{ref} $$
∑ Why 4095 and not 4096?

The codes are 0 … 4095 — that's 4096 distinct values, but the largest code is 4095. Full scale (code 4095) corresponds to one LSB below $V_{ref}$. So we divide by the maximum code, 4095, to map code → voltage. Dividing by 4096 instead introduces a tiny systematic bias of one LSB at full scale; for a quick estimate it barely matters, but for calibrated measurements, use 4095.

Worked example: the ADC returns adc = 2048, the midpoint. Then

$$ V = \frac{2048}{4095}\times 3.3\,\text{V} \approx 1.65\,\text{V} $$

— exactly half of $V_{ref}$, as you'd expect from the midpoint code. Compare with the old 10-bit AVR: there, $\text{LSB} = 5\,\text{V}/1024 \approx 4.9\,\text{mV}$ — six times coarser than the STM32's 0.806 mV. Twelve bits buys you real precision.

3Conversion timing & the ADC clock

② Math how long a sample takes

A SAR (successive-approximation) ADC works like a binary search: it guesses the most-significant bit, compares, keeps or discards it, then the next bit, and so on — one comparison per bit. Before any of that, it must sample: connect the input to an internal capacitor and let it charge to the source voltage. A high-impedance sensor charges that cap slowly, so the sampling time is programmable.

On the STM32F4, total conversion time (in ADC clock cycles) is:

$$ T_{conv} = \frac{T_{sampling} + 12.5}{f_{ADC}} $$

Here $T_{sampling}$ is the chosen sample time in ADC cycles (the F4 offers 3, 15, 28, 56, 84, 112, 144, or 480 cycles), and the fixed $12.5$ cycles is the successive-approximation phase for a 12-bit result (12 bits + 0.5 cycle of overhead). The denominator $f_{ADC}$ is the ADC's own clock.

🔑 The ADC clock has a hard ceiling

On the F4, the ADC is clocked from APB2 through the ADCPRE prescaler (÷2, ÷4, ÷6, ÷8). The resulting $f_{ADC}$ must not exceed the datasheet maximum — typically ~36 MHz on F4 parts. Overclock it and conversions silently lose accuracy. Always pick a prescaler that keeps $f_{ADC}$ under the max.

Worked example: APB2 at 84 MHz, prescaler ÷4 gives $f_{ADC} = 21\,\text{MHz}$ (safely under 36 MHz). With a 15-cycle sample time:

$$ T_{conv} = \frac{15 + 12.5}{21\times10^{6}\,\text{Hz}} = \frac{27.5}{21\times10^{6}} \approx 1.31\,\mu\text{s} \;\Rightarrow\; \approx 763\,\text{kSPS} $$
① Intuition four ways to schedule conversions

The ADC doesn't just convert once and stop. It has four modes, and picking the right one is half the design:

ModeWhat it doesUse when
SingleOne channel, one conversion, then stop.Occasional spot reads (battery voltage at boot).
ContinuousOne channel, convert → restart → convert, forever.Streaming one signal as fast as possible.
ScanWalk a sequence of channels once (or continuously).Reading several sensors as a group.
InjectedA high-priority sequence that pre-empts the regular one.An urgent reading (motor current) interrupting a slow scan.

Scan + continuous + DMA is the combination that turns the ADC into the conveyor belt from §1: it walks your channel list over and over, and DMA catches every result. That's next.

4The superpower: ADC + DMA streaming

① Intuition the conveyor belt

DMA (Direct Memory Access) is a tiny dedicated copier that moves data between a peripheral and RAM without the CPU. Wire the ADC's data register to a DMA channel and every finished conversion is whisked into your buffer automatically. Set the DMA to circular mode and it wraps to the start when the buffer fills — an endless ring the ADC keeps filling and you keep reading.

flowchart LR PIN["Analog pins
PA0 · PA1 · PA4"] --> ADC["ADC1
scan + continuous"] ADC -->|each result| DMA["DMA2 Stream0
circular"] DMA -->|writes| BUF["RAM buffer
uint16_t[N]"] BUF -.->|CPU reads
at its leisure| CPU(["Control loop"]) ADC -. zero CPU per sample .-> CPU
💡 Why this is the whole point

Without DMA, every conversion is a poll or an interrupt — the CPU stops to fetch one number, thousands of times a second. With DMA, the CPU configures the chain once, then the ADC and DMA run as a closed loop. The CPU only touches the buffer when it wants a fresh reading. This is how you sample sensors at hundreds of kSPS while still running PID, comms, and a UI.

③ Code read every line

Here's a realistic HAL configuration: three channels (PA0, PA1, PA4) scanned continuously, streamed circularly into a buffer. The ADC restarts the scan the instant DMA finishes a pass.

adc_dma.c C / STM32 HAL
#define NUM_CH   3
#define NUM_AVG  16                 // optional: keep several scans
volatile uint16_t adc_buf[NUM_CH]; // DMA writes here, in channel order

extern ADC_HandleTypeDef hadc1;    // from CubeMX / your init

void adc_dma_start(void) {
  // Kick off the chain. HAL programs the DMA stream and the ADC,
  // then ADC conversions stream straight into adc_buf with ZERO CPU.
  // Cast: HAL wants uint32_t*, our data is 16-bit half-words (set in CubeMX).
  HAL_ADC_Start_DMA(&hadc1, (uint32_t *)adc_buf, NUM_CH);
}

// Called by HAL from the DMA transfer-complete ISR, once per full scan.
// Because DMA is CIRCULAR, conversions never stop — this just notifies us.
void HAL_ADC_ConvCpltCallback(ADC_HandleTypeDef *hadc) {
  if (hadc->Instance == ADC1) {
    // adc_buf now holds one fresh sample per channel. Convert as needed.
    // (Reading is safe here; DMA is between passes.)
    float v0 = (adc_buf[0] / 4095.0f) * 3.3f;   // PA0 → volts
    (void)v0;                                    // ... use it
  }
}

The matching low-level setup CubeMX generates is worth seeing once, because it's where the policy lives — scan, continuous, DMA-circular all encoded as register fields:

adc_init.c C / STM32 HAL
void MX_ADC1_Init(void) {
  hadc1.Instance = ADC1;
  hadc1.Init.ClockPrescaler   = ADC_CLOCK_SYNC_PCLK_DIV4; // f_ADC = APB2/4
  hadc1.Init.Resolution       = ADC_RESOLUTION_12B;       // 12 bits → 0..4095
  hadc1.Init.ScanConvMode     = ENABLE;     // walk the channel sequence
  hadc1.Init.ContinuousConvMode = ENABLE;   // restart the scan forever
  hadc1.Init.NbrOfConversion  = 3;          // three channels in the list
  hadc1.Init.DMAContinuousRequests = ENABLE;// keep DMA requests coming
  hadc1.Init.EOCSelection     = ADC_EOC_SINGLE_CONV;
  HAL_ADC_Init(&hadc1);

  ADC_ChannelConfTypeDef sc = {0};
  sc.SamplingTime = ADC_SAMPLETIME_15CYCLES;
  sc.Channel = ADC_CHANNEL_0; sc.Rank = 1; HAL_ADC_ConfigChannel(&hadc1, &sc);
  sc.Channel = ADC_CHANNEL_1; sc.Rank = 2; HAL_ADC_ConfigChannel(&hadc1, &sc);
  sc.Channel = ADC_CHANNEL_4; sc.Rank = 3; HAL_ADC_ConfigChannel(&hadc1, &sc);
}
⚠️ Pitfall — the buffer must match the sequence

DMA writes results in rank order, not pin order. The Rank field decides which slot each channel lands in: rank 1 → adc_buf[0], rank 2 → adc_buf[1], and so on. Get the ranks wrong and your "PA4 reading" is silently PA1. Also: mark the buffer volatile — DMA changes it behind the compiler's back, and without volatile the optimizer may cache a stale value.

5Going the other way: the DAC

① Intuition numbers become voltage

A DAC (digital-to-analog converter) is the ADC run backwards: write a number, get a voltage. Many STM32 parts (F4, L4, etc.) include one or two 12-bit DACs. Write a 12-bit code to the data register and the output pin holds a steady analog voltage — perfect for generating waveforms, audio, control offsets, or a reference for an external circuit.

The mapping mirrors the ADC's. A code of 0 gives ~0 V; the maximum code 4095 gives full scale near $V_{ref}$:

$$ V_{out} = \frac{\text{val}}{4095}\, V_{ref} $$

So to put 1.0 V on the pin with $V_{ref} = 3.3\,\text{V}$, solve for the code: $\text{val} = \dfrac{1.0}{3.3}\times 4095 \approx 1241$.

③ Code set an output voltage
dac_out.c C / STM32 HAL
extern DAC_HandleTypeDef hdac;

void dac_set_volts(float volts) {
  // Clamp to the legal range, then map volts → 12-bit code.
  if (volts < 0.0f)  volts = 0.0f;
  if (volts > 3.3f)  volts = 3.3f;
  uint32_t code = (uint32_t)((volts / 3.3f) * 4095.0f + 0.5f); // round

  HAL_DAC_SetValue(&hdac, DAC_CHANNEL_1, DAC_ALIGN_12B_R, code);
  HAL_DAC_Start(&hdac, DAC_CHANNEL_1);
}
🛠️ DAC + DMA + timer = a waveform generator

The real trick: pre-compute a sine table in RAM, then have a timer trigger the DAC and DMA feed it the next table entry on every tick. The CPU writes the table once and the hardware plays it forever — a clean analog sine on the pin with zero CPU load. Same conveyor-belt pattern as the ADC, just flowing outward.

6Oversampling: buying extra bits

① Intuition average away the noise

Your 12-bit reading isn't perfectly stable — the last bit or two flickers from thermal and quantization noise. That flicker is annoying, but it's also a gift: if the noise is roughly random and at least one LSB wide, averaging many samples recovers information below one LSB. Average enough and you genuinely resolve finer than 12 bits. This is oversampling.

② Math derive the bit gain

Averaging $N$ independent samples reduces the noise standard deviation by $\sqrt{N}$:

$$ \sigma_{avg} = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{N}} $$

Each extra bit of resolution halves the effective step size — i.e. needs the noise floor to drop by a factor of 2. Since averaging drops noise by $\sqrt{N}$, to gain $n$ bits you need $\sqrt{N} = 2^{n}$, so $N = 4^{n}$. The clean rule:

$$ \Delta\text{ENOB} = \frac{1}{2}\log_{2} N = \log_{4} N \quad\Longrightarrow\quad N = 4^{\,n}\ \text{samples}\ \to\ +n\ \text{bits} $$
∑ The trade table

Each extra effective bit costs 4× the samples (and 4× the time):

Samples averaged $N$Extra bits $n$Effective resolutionThroughput cost
$4^1 = 4$+113-bit÷4
$4^2 = 16$+214-bit÷16
$4^3 = 64$+315-bit÷64
$4^4 = 256$+416-bit÷256

So 16 samples averaged turn a 12-bit ADC into an effective 14-bit one — $\text{ENOB} = 12 + \log_4 16 = 12 + 2 = 14$ bits. The catch is throughput: you've spent 16 conversions per usable reading, so your output rate drops by 16×. That's the eternal embedded trade — resolution vs. speed — and now you can put numbers on it.

⚠️ Pitfall — oversampling needs noise to work

If the signal is perfectly quiet (noise < 1 LSB), averaging just returns the same code every time — no extra bits, because there's nothing for the average to interpolate. Oversampling only buys resolution when there's at least ~1 LSB of random dither riding on the signal. Real-world thermal noise usually provides it; on a bench-perfect source you may need to add deliberate dither.

🛠️ Hardware does it for free on newer parts

STM32L4/G4/H7 ADCs include a built-in hardware oversampler: set an oversampling ratio and a right-shift, and the ADC sums $N$ conversions and shifts the result — no CPU averaging, no extra firmware. On the F4 you do it in software (sum the DMA buffer, shift right). Either way the math above is what's happening.

7The analog watchdog & a real system

① Intuition a hardware alarm

Sometimes you don't want the value — you want to know the instant a value leaves a safe band. Polling for that in software wastes cycles and adds latency. The analog watchdog is hardware that does it: program a low threshold and a high threshold, and the ADC fires an interrupt the moment a conversion falls outside $[V_{low}, V_{high}]$. No polling, near-zero latency.

🔑 Thresholds are in ADC codes, not volts

You set the watchdog limits as raw codes. To alarm above 2.0 V at $V_{ref}=3.3$V: $\text{HT} = \dfrac{2.0}{3.3}\times 4095 \approx 2482$. Convert your physical limits to codes the same way you convert readings back to volts — it's the inverse of the §2 formula.

④ Real case this ships in products
🏭 Real case — a motor controller that never blocks on the ADC

A brushless-motor board needs to read bus voltage, board temperature, and a throttle pot — continuously — while running a 20 kHz control loop. The design: ADC1 in scan + continuous mode walks all three channels into a 3-word circular DMA buffer. The control loop reads adc_buf[] whenever it likes; the values are always fresh, and not a single CPU cycle is spent waiting on a conversion. An analog watchdog on the bus-voltage channel is armed to fault-trip the motor instantly if the supply sags below a safe code — a hardware safety interlock, faster and more reliable than any software check. This exact pattern is in countless shipped drives, drones, and power supplies.

Notice every piece you learned snapping together: 12-bit resolution sets precision, the conversion-timing budget sets how fast the scan loops, scan mode picks the channels, DMA-circular removes the CPU from the loop, and the watchdog adds a hardware safety net. That's a complete analog front-end.

Q1 Conceptual check
You're sampling four sensors at high rate while running a control loop. Why pair the ADC with DMA in circular mode instead of reading each conversion in an interrupt?
  • DMA makes each individual conversion physically faster than the SAR core can run.
  • DMA moves every result into a RAM buffer with zero CPU per sample, so the CPU is free to run the control loop and only touches the buffer when it wants a fresh reading.
  • DMA increases the ADC's resolution from 12 bits to 16 bits.
  • Without DMA the ADC cannot do multi-channel scan mode at all.
DMA doesn't speed up the converter itself — the SAR timing is fixed by $T_{conv}$. What it removes is the per-sample CPU cost: each finished conversion is copied to RAM by the DMA engine, and circular mode means it loops forever without re-arming. The CPU configures the chain once and then ignores it, reading the buffer at its own pace. That's how you sample continuously and still have CPU left for everything else.

Hint
Three Init flags do the heavy lifting: ScanConvMode = ENABLE, ContinuousConvMode = ENABLE, and DMAContinuousRequests = ENABLE. Set NbrOfConversion = 2 and give each channel a Rank. Show one solution
adc2ch_dma.c C / STM32 HAL
volatile uint16_t buf[2];          // DMA writes here; volatile is mandatory

void MX_ADC1_Init(void) {
  hadc1.Instance = ADC1;
  hadc1.Init.ClockPrescaler   = ADC_CLOCK_SYNC_PCLK_DIV4; // keep f_ADC <= 36 MHz
  hadc1.Init.Resolution       = ADC_RESOLUTION_12B;
  hadc1.Init.ScanConvMode     = ENABLE;     // (a) walk the 2-channel list
  hadc1.Init.ContinuousConvMode = ENABLE;   // (b) restart the scan forever
  hadc1.Init.DMAContinuousRequests = ENABLE;// (c) keep DMA requests flowing
  hadc1.Init.NbrOfConversion  = 2;          // two ranks in the sequence
  hadc1.Init.EOCSelection     = ADC_EOC_SINGLE_CONV;
  HAL_ADC_Init(&hadc1);

  ADC_ChannelConfTypeDef sc = {0};
  sc.SamplingTime = ADC_SAMPLETIME_15CYCLES;
  sc.Channel = ADC_CHANNEL_0; sc.Rank = 1; HAL_ADC_ConfigChannel(&hadc1, &sc); // PA0 → buf[0]
  sc.Channel = ADC_CHANNEL_1; sc.Rank = 2; HAL_ADC_ConfigChannel(&hadc1, &sc); // PA1 → buf[1]
}

void start(void) {
  // The DMA stream is set to CIRCULAR in CubeMX, so this never has to be
  // re-armed: conversions stream into buf[] endlessly with zero CPU.
  HAL_ADC_Start_DMA(&hadc1, (uint32_t *)buf, 2);
}

The three ENABLEs are the scan/continuous/DMA trio; Rank maps each pin to its buffer slot; circular DMA (a stream setting, not an ADC field) makes it perpetual; and volatile stops the compiler from caching stale buffer reads. Drop any one of these and the stream stalls, misorders, or reads garbage — the four classic ADC-DMA bugs.

🎓 Chapter recap

You can now convert between ADC codes and volts ($V = \tfrac{\text{adc}}{4095}V_{ref}$, LSB $\approx 0.806$ mV at 3.3 V), budget conversion time from $T_{conv} = (T_{sampling}+12.5)/f_{ADC}$ while respecting the ~36 MHz F4 ADC-clock ceiling, choose between single / continuous / scan / injected modes, and — the headline — stream multiple channels into a circular RAM buffer with HAL_ADC_Start_DMA so the CPU spends zero cycles per sample. You can drive a DAC ($V_{out} = \tfrac{\text{val}}{4095}V_{ref}$), trade throughput for resolution with oversampling ($N = 4^n$ samples → $+n$ effective bits, $\sqrt{N}$ noise reduction), and arm an analog watchdog as a hardware safety alarm.
