Sensors & Actuators: Real-World Interfacing

Until now your pins talked to LEDs and other pins. Now they meet the messy physical world — distance, temperature, mains-powered fans, spinning motors. The world fights back with noise, voltage spikes, and back-EMF. This chapter is about connecting to it reliably, so your robot survives its first collision and your relay board survives its first switch-off.

⏱ ~50 min🧠 Signal chain⚡ HC-SR04 · DHT · H-bridge🔧 Flyback & isolation

1The signal chain: sensors measure, actuators act

① Intuition build the picture first

Every embedded system that touches the real world is a signal chain: the physical world is sensed, turned into a voltage, conditioned, read by the MCU, decided upon, and then pushed back out to an actuator that changes the world. Sensors are the chain's input half; actuators are the output half. Your firmware is the thin layer of judgment in the middle.

💡 The one-sentence model

A sensor converts a physical quantity into an electrical signal the MCU can read; an actuator converts an electrical signal the MCU emits back into a physical effect. Everything in this chapter is one of those two, plus the discipline to wire them so they don't destroy each other.

Two ideas dominate whether a connection works at all. The first is voltage level: an ATmega328P pin is happy at 0–5 V and is destroyed above ~5.5 V; a 3.3 V board like an ESP32 dies above ~3.6 V. The second is impedance — loosely, how much current a node can source or sink. A sensor output may be a weak, high-impedance signal that a long noisy wire corrupts; an MCU pin can only source ~20–40 mA, nowhere near enough to spin a motor. The whole craft is matching levels and impedances so each stage can actually drive the next.

🔑 The pin is not a power supply

An ATmega328P I/O pin is rated for ~20 mA continuous (40 mA absolute max), with a per-chip total around 200 mA. An LED is fine. A relay coil (~70 mA), a servo, or a DC motor (hundreds of mA to amps) is not — those need a driver transistor or dedicated chip between the pin and the load. Drive a motor straight off a pin and you will brown out the chip or burn the pin.

2Measuring distance: the HC-SR04 & the speed of sound

① Intuition echolocation in a $2 module

The HC-SR04 is a sonar. You poke its TRIG pin with a brief pulse; it emits a 40 kHz ultrasonic "chirp," then raises its ECHO pin HIGH and holds it high until the chirp bounces off something and returns. The width of that ECHO pulse is the round-trip flight time of sound. Time it, and you know how far away the obstacle is — just like a bat.

sequenceDiagram participant MCU participant HCSR04 as HC-SR04 participant Obj as Obstacle MCU->>HCSR04: TRIG HIGH for 10 µs HCSR04->>Obj: 8 × 40 kHz burst HCSR04->>MCU: ECHO goes HIGH Obj-->>HCSR04: sound reflects back HCSR04->>MCU: ECHO goes LOW (after t_echo) Note over MCU: pulse width = round-trip time
② Math derive the distance formula

Sound travels at roughly $v \approx 343\ \text{m/s}$ in dry air at 20 °C. The ECHO pulse measures the time for the chirp to fly out to the obstacle and back — twice the distance. So the one-way distance is half the flight path:

$$ d = \frac{v \cdot t_{\text{echo}}}{2} $$

Now make it practical. The Arduino measures $t_{\text{echo}}$ in microseconds, and we want $d$ in centimetres. Convert the speed of sound into cm/µs:

$$ v = 343\ \tfrac{\text{m}}{\text{s}} = 343\times\frac{100\ \text{cm}}{10^{6}\ \mu\text{s}} = 0.0343\ \tfrac{\text{cm}}{\mu\text{s}} $$

Substitute into the distance formula and collect the constants:

$$ d_{\text{cm}} = \frac{0.0343 \cdot t_{\mu s}}{2} = \frac{t_{\mu s}}{58.3} \;\approx\; \frac{t_{\mu s}}{58} $$

That is the famous "divide by 58" rule every robotics tutorial quotes. It is just $2/0.0343 = 58.3$, rounded. Using 58 instead of 58.3 over-reports distance by about 0.5% — negligible next to the temperature dependence of $v$ itself, which drifts ~0.17% per °C. If you need accuracy, compute $v = 331.4 + 0.6\,T_{°C}$ m/s and divide by the matching constant.

∑ Worked example

ECHO stays HIGH for $t_{\text{echo}} = 1740\ \mu\text{s}$. Then $d = 1740 / 58 = 30\ \text{cm}$. Sanity check with SI: $d = 0.0343 \times 1740 / 2 = 29.9\ \text{cm}$. The obstacle is about 30 cm away.

③ Code read every line

pulseIn(pin, HIGH) is the perfect tool here: it blocks until the pin goes HIGH, starts a microsecond timer, and returns the pulse width in µs when the pin goes LOW again. We give it a timeout so a missing echo (object too far, or no object) doesn't hang the loop forever.

hcsr04.ino C++ / Arduino
const uint8_t TRIG = 9;
const uint8_t ECHO = 10;

void setup() {
  Serial.begin(115200);
  pinMode(TRIG, OUTPUT);
  pinMode(ECHO, INPUT);
  digitalWrite(TRIG, LOW);   // ensure a clean LOW before the first pulse
}

float measureCm() {
  // 1) Fire a clean 10 us trigger pulse.
  digitalWrite(TRIG, LOW);  delayMicroseconds(2);
  digitalWrite(TRIG, HIGH); delayMicroseconds(10);   // datasheet: >= 10 us
  digitalWrite(TRIG, LOW);

  // 2) Time how long ECHO stays HIGH (round-trip flight time, in us).
  //    Timeout 30000 us caps range at ~5 m and prevents a hang.
  unsigned long t = pulseIn(ECHO, HIGH, 30000UL);
  if (t == 0) return -1.0;          // no echo within timeout

  // 3) Convert: d_cm = t_us / 58.3  (= half of 0.0343 cm/us round trip).
  return t / 58.3f;
}

void loop() {
  float cm = measureCm();
  if (cm < 0) Serial.println(F("out of range"));
  else { Serial.print(cm, 1); Serial.println(F(" cm")); }
  delay(60);                        // >= 60 ms between pings: avoid hearing
}                                   // the previous chirp's late echoes
⚠️ Pitfall — the 5 V ECHO line on a 3.3 V board

The classic HC-SR04 runs on 5 V and drives ECHO to 5 V. Feed that straight into a 3.3 V ESP32 or STM32 pin and you slowly cook it. Drop ECHO with a resistor divider (e.g. 1 kΩ in series, 2 kΩ to ground gives $5\cdot\tfrac{2}{3}=3.3$ V) or a level shifter. TRIG is an input on the module, so the 3.3 V HIGH it receives is fine.

🔬 Live: echo time → distance

Drag the ECHO pulse width and watch the obstacle move. The readout applies d = t/58.3 in real time — exactly what your firmware computes.

3One-wire timing: the DHT11/DHT22 protocol

① Intuition a whole conversation on one wire

The HC-SR04 hands you an analog-ish pulse width. Many digital sensors instead speak a bit-banged serial protocol over a single data line, where the duration of each HIGH pulse encodes a bit. The DHT11 (temperature + humidity, cheap, low resolution) and DHT22 (a.k.a. AM2302, finer resolution) are the canonical example, and they teach the pattern you'll see in WS2812 LEDs and many others.

🔑 The DHT handshake, in words

1) The MCU pulls the line LOW for ≥18 ms (DHT11) to wake the sensor, then releases it. 2) The sensor answers with an 80 µs LOW then 80 µs HIGH. 3) It streams 40 bits (16 humidity, 16 temperature, 8 checksum). Each bit starts with a 50 µs LOW; then a ~26 µs HIGH means 0, a ~70 µs HIGH means 1. You decode by timing the HIGH.

sequenceDiagram participant MCU participant DHT MCU->>DHT: pull LOW >= 18 ms (start) MCU->>DHT: release (pull-up takes line HIGH) DHT->>MCU: 80 us LOW + 80 us HIGH (present) loop 40 bits DHT->>MCU: 50 us LOW (bit start) DHT->>MCU: HIGH ~26us = 0 / ~70us = 1 end Note over MCU: byte5 == (b0+b1+b2+b3) & 0xFF ? valid

The timing is so tight (microseconds) that reading a DHT reliably means disabling interrupts during the capture, which is exactly why you use a vetted library rather than hand-rolling it. But you must understand the protocol to debug it: a checksum failure usually means an interrupt (a millis tick, a serial byte) stretched one of those HIGH windows and flipped a bit.

② Math the checksum

The 40 bits arrive as five bytes. The last is a checksum: the low 8 bits of the sum of the first four. You verify with

$$ \text{checksum} \;\overset{?}{=}\; (b_0 + b_1 + b_2 + b_3)\ \&\ \texttt{0xFF} $$
dht_decode.ino C++ / Arduino
// Conceptual decode of the 5 captured bytes (a real driver captures the
// 40 edge timings first; here we show only the validation + scaling).
bool dhtDecode(const uint8_t b[5], float &rh, float &tC) {
  uint8_t sum = b[0] + b[1] + b[2] + b[3];   // 8-bit wrap is intentional
  if (sum != b[4]) return false;             // checksum mismatch -> reject

  // DHT22: 16-bit values, 0.1 unit resolution, MSB of temp = sign.
  rh = ((b[0] << 8) | b[1]) * 0.1f;
  int16_t raw = ((b[2] & 0x7F) << 8) | b[3];
  tC = raw * 0.1f;
  if (b[2] & 0x80) tC = -tC;                  // top bit set => negative
  return true;
}
⚠️ Pitfall — sampling a DHT too fast

A DHT11 updates at ~1 Hz, a DHT22 at ~0.5 Hz. Poll faster and you get stale or corrupt reads. Also: the data line needs a 4.7–10 kΩ pull-up to VCC, because the protocol relies on the line floating HIGH when neither side drives it. Forget the pull-up and you get all-zero or all-one garbage.

4Actuators & the flyback diode: taming inductive kick

① Intuition why coils bite back

Relays, solenoids, and motors all contain a coil — an inductor. An inductor hates a sudden change in current: its whole nature is to keep current flowing. So the instant you switch one OFF, the magnetic field collapses and the coil generates a large reverse voltage spike (back-EMF) trying to maintain that current. That spike can reach hundreds of volts and will arc across your switching transistor or weld your relay contacts.

② Math the inductor's revenge

The voltage across an inductor is proportional to how fast its current changes:

$$ v_L = L\,\frac{di}{dt} $$

When a transistor switches a coil off, it tries to drop the current from its running value to zero in nanoseconds. With $dt \to 0$, that derivative explodes and $v_L$ spikes enormous. A flyback diode (a.k.a. freewheeling diode) wired across the coil, reverse-biased during normal operation, gives that trapped current a harmless loop to circulate in when the switch opens. The current now decays gently as

$$ i(t) = I_0\,e^{-t/\tau}, \qquad \tau = \frac{L}{R} $$

and the spike is clamped to roughly one diode drop (~0.7 V) above the supply instead of hundreds of volts. One cheap 1N4007 across every coil. This is non-negotiable.

🔑 Diode orientation — get this right

The diode's cathode (banded end) goes to +V, its anode to the transistor/coil junction. So during normal operation it is reverse-biased and invisible. Only when the coil flings its voltage above +V does it conduct, shunting the current in a circle. Install it backwards and you short your supply through a forward diode — instant smoke.

🛠️ AC loads need a snubber, not a diode

A flyback diode only works for DC coils. If your relay contacts switch an inductive AC load (a mains motor, a transformer), the arc is on the contact side and a diode can't help — you use an RC snubber (e.g. 100 Ω + 100 nF) or a varistor across the contacts to absorb the arc energy and stop contact erosion.

5Relays, opto-isolation & switching dangerous loads

① Intuition a tiny signal commanding a big one

A relay is an electrically operated switch: a small coil current pulls a mechanical contact closed, letting a completely separate (and possibly mains-voltage) circuit conduct. Its superpower is galvanic isolation — the 5 V control side shares no copper with the 230 V load side, so a fault on the load can't fry your MCU. The standard hobby relay board adds an opto-coupler so even the coil-driver transistor is optically isolated from your microcontroller: the only thing crossing the boundary is light.

③ Code active-low boards bite beginners

Most hobby relay boards are active-LOW: the relay energizes when you drive the input pin LOW, because the opto-coupler's LED is wired between VCC and your pin. This also means the relay may click ON at boot, while the pin floats — set the pin HIGH (de-energized) before setting it OUTPUT to avoid a glitch.

relay.ino C++ / Arduino
const uint8_t RELAY = 7;   // active-LOW opto board: LOW = ON, HIGH = OFF

void setup() {
  digitalWrite(RELAY, HIGH);   // de-energized FIRST, before pinMode...
  pinMode(RELAY, OUTPUT);      // ...so we don't click ON during boot
}

inline void relayOn()  { digitalWrite(RELAY, LOW);  }   // note: inverted
inline void relayOff() { digitalWrite(RELAY, HIGH); }

void loop() {
  relayOn();  delay(2000);     // load powered for 2 s
  relayOff(); delay(2000);     // and off for 2 s
}
🏭 Real case — when isolation is the whole point

In an industrial PLC, the 24 V logic and the 400 V three-phase motor it commands are deliberately kept on separate ground planes joined only through opto-couplers and relay contacts. The reason is brutal and concrete: a motor fault can dump a kilovolt transient onto its wiring, and the isolation barrier is the only thing standing between that transient and a $50,000 controller. The same principle scales down to your $3 relay board — never let mains and logic share copper.

⚠️ Pitfall — mains is not a tutorial

230 V / 120 V mains can kill you. If you are not certain of creepage distances, enclosure, fusing, and earthing, switch a low-voltage DC load (a 12 V fan, an LED strip) for practice and leave mains to qualified work. The firmware is identical; the consequences of a slip are not.

6DC motors & the H-bridge: direction + speed

① Intuition four switches that reverse a motor

A DC motor spins one way when current flows through it, the other way when current reverses. To reverse current from a single supply you need an H-bridge: four switches arranged like the letter H around the motor. Close the top-left + bottom-right pair and current flows one way; close top-right + bottom-left and it flows the other. A driver chip like the L298N (older, BJT, ~1.7 V drop, up to 2 A/channel) or the far more efficient DRV8833 (MOSFET, low drop, up to ~1.5 A/channel) packages two H-bridges so you can drive two motors.

⚠️ Pitfall — shoot-through

Never close both switches on the same side of the H (e.g. top-left + bottom-left). That shorts the supply straight to ground through two transistors — "shoot-through" — and destroys the bridge in microseconds. The forbidden input combination is the reason driver chips exist: they enforce dead-time and reject illegal states for you.

② Math speed from PWM duty

The H-bridge sets direction; PWM (Chapter 4) on the enable pin sets speed. A DC motor's average applied voltage is the supply scaled by the duty cycle, and to a first approximation its no-load speed is proportional to that average voltage:

$$ \bar{V}_{\text{motor}} = D \cdot V_{\text{supply}}, \qquad \omega \;\approx\; \frac{\bar{V}_{\text{motor}} - I\,R_a}{k_e} $$

where $D$ is duty (0–1), $k_e$ is the motor's back-EMF constant, and $I R_a$ is the armature-resistance drop. The takeaway: duty cycle is your throttle. At $D = 0.5$ on a 12 V supply the motor sees ~6 V average and runs near half its no-load speed (under load it's softer because of the $IR_a$ term). The H-bridge transistors must also be PWM-friendly, which is why we switch the enable line, not the direction lines.

③ Code direction + speed with a DRV8833-style driver

Here we use the L298N convention: two direction inputs (IN1, IN2) set polarity, and a PWM-capable ENA pin sets speed via analogWrite. The truth table below the code is the whole mental model.

hbridge.ino C++ / Arduino
const uint8_t IN1 = 4, IN2 = 5;   // direction control
const uint8_t ENA = 6;            // PWM speed (pin 6 = Timer0 OC0A on Uno)

void setup() {
  pinMode(IN1, OUTPUT); pinMode(IN2, OUTPUT); pinMode(ENA, OUTPUT);
}

// speed: 0..255 PWM duty; dir: +1 forward, -1 reverse, 0 = coast/brake
void drive(int speed, int dir) {
  if (dir > 0)      { digitalWrite(IN1, HIGH); digitalWrite(IN2, LOW);  }
  else if (dir < 0) { digitalWrite(IN1, LOW);  digitalWrite(IN2, HIGH); }
  else              { digitalWrite(IN1, LOW);  digitalWrite(IN2, LOW);  } // coast
  analogWrite(ENA, constrain(speed, 0, 255));   // duty = speed/255
}

void loop() {
  drive(200, +1); delay(1500);   // forward, ~78% duty
  drive(0,    0); delay(400);    // coast
  drive(120, -1); delay(1500);   // reverse, ~47% duty
  drive(0,    0); delay(400);
}

🔑 Servos vs. DC motors vs. steppers

Hobby servo (Chapter on PWM): you command an angle with a 1–2 ms pulse every 20 ms; it has its own internal H-bridge and feedback. DC motor: you command direction + speed; you supply the H-bridge. Stepper: discrete steps via a sequenced multi-coil driver (A4988/DRV8825). Different jobs, different drivers — but every one of them needs flyback protection because every one is a coil.

7Power discipline: separate supplies, common ground

① Intuition why your MCU resets when the motor starts

The single most common beginner failure: the robot drives forward and the Arduino reboots. Cause: the motor's inrush current (it can briefly pull 5–10× its running current at stall/startup) collapses the shared 5 V rail below the brown-out threshold, and the MCU resets. The fix is power discipline.

🔑 The three rules of mixed-signal power

1. Separate the motor supply. Run motors/relays from their own battery or regulator, never through the MCU's 5 V pin.
2. Common ground. All supplies' grounds must be tied together — otherwise control signals have no shared reference and the H-bridge reads garbage. This is the rule beginners skip and then can't explain why nothing works.
3. Decouple everything. A 100 nF ceramic close to each chip's VCC, plus a bulk 100–470 µF electrolytic across the motor supply, to absorb switching transients.

flowchart TB BAT["Battery / motor supply"] --> REG["5V regulator"] REG --> MCU["MCU VCC"] BAT --> DRV["Motor driver VM"] MCU -. "signal" .-> DRV GND([COMMON GROUND]) --- MCU GND --- DRV GND --- BAT
② Math budgeting current

Add up worst-case currents before you choose a supply. For a two-motor robot: two motors at stall, a sonar, and the MCU:

$$ I_{\text{total}} = \sum_k I_k = 2\,(0.8\,\text{A}) + 0.015\,\text{A} + 0.05\,\text{A} \approx 1.67\,\text{A} $$

Pick a supply rated comfortably above that — say 2.5 A — so transients don't sag the rail. Then size your wires and the driver's current rating to the same budget. An L298N caps around 2 A/channel and wastes ~1.7 V per channel as heat; a DRV8833 keeps far more of your battery in the motor. Current budgeting is the difference between a robot that runs all afternoon and one that resets every time it turns.

8Real case: an obstacle-avoiding robot

④ Real case this is the whole chapter in one loop

Everything above composes into a tiny autonomous robot: an HC-SR04 looks ahead, the firmware decides, and an H-bridge steers two DC motors. Sense → decide → act — the signal chain from §1, made real.

avoider.ino C++ / Arduino
// Pins: sonar on 9/10, left motor IN1/IN2 + ENL, right motor IN3/IN4 + ENR.
const uint8_t TRIG=9, ECHO=10;
const uint8_t IN1=4, IN2=5, ENL=6, IN3=7, IN4=8, ENR=11;
const float   STOP_CM = 25.0f;     // turn away if obstacle closer than this

float ping() {
  digitalWrite(TRIG, LOW);  delayMicroseconds(2);
  digitalWrite(TRIG, HIGH); delayMicroseconds(10);
  digitalWrite(TRIG, LOW);
  unsigned long t = pulseIn(ECHO, HIGH, 30000UL);
  return t ? t / 58.3f : 999.0f;   // no echo => treat as "far"
}

void wheels(int l, int lDir, int r, int rDir) {
  digitalWrite(IN1, lDir > 0); digitalWrite(IN2, lDir < 0); analogWrite(ENL, l);
  digitalWrite(IN3, rDir > 0); digitalWrite(IN4, rDir < 0); analogWrite(ENR, r);
}

void setup() {
  pinMode(TRIG, OUTPUT); pinMode(ECHO, INPUT);
  uint8_t outs[] = {IN1,IN2,ENL,IN3,IN4,ENR};
  for (uint8_t p : outs) pinMode(p, OUTPUT);
}

void loop() {
  if (ping() < STOP_CM) {           // obstacle ahead: spin in place to the right
    wheels(180, +1, 180, -1);
    delay(350);
  } else {                          // clear: drive forward at ~70% duty
    wheels(180, +1, 180, +1);
  }
  delay(60);                        // honor the >=60 ms sonar spacing
}
🏭 Real case — this pattern ships in real products

A robot vacuum is exactly this loop scaled up: cliff sensors and bumpers feed a decision layer that commands a dual H-bridge for the drive wheels and a brushless driver for the suction motor — each inductive load with its own freewheeling protection, all on a star-grounded power tree fed from one battery through separate regulators. The $20 desk robot and the $400 vacuum run the same skeleton you just read. Master the skeleton and you can build either.

Q1 Conceptual check
You wire a DC motor to an external 9 V battery through an H-bridge, with the Arduino on its own 5 V supply. The motor twitches randomly and the bridge reads garbage commands. You add a flyback diode and it still misbehaves. What is the most likely remaining mistake?
  • The flyback diode is too small — use a bigger one
  • The two supplies' grounds aren't tied together (no common ground)
  • The motor needs a higher PWM frequency
  • The Arduino pin can't source enough current for the motor
With separate supplies you must join their grounds. Logic signals are voltages relative to ground; without a shared reference the driver can't tell HIGH from LOW and reads garbage. The flyback diode protects against back-EMF (a different problem), and the Arduino pin only drives the driver's logic input, not the motor — so common ground is the missing piece.
Q2 Compute it
Your HC-SR04's ECHO pulse measures 1740 µs wide. How far away is the obstacle, in centimetres? (Use the rule $d_{\text{cm}} = t_{\mu s}/58$.)
cm
$d = 1740 / 58 = 30\ \text{cm}$. Cross-check in SI: $d = v\,t/2 = 0.0343\,(\text{cm}/\mu s) \times 1740\,\mu s / 2 = 29.9\ \text{cm}$. The "/58" rule is just $2/0.0343$, rounded — fast enough to run every loop.
Q3 Coding challenge — distance with pulseIn
Write a function float distanceCm() for an HC-SR04 on TRIG = 3, ECHO = 2. It must fire a proper 10 µs trigger pulse, time the echo with a timeout, and return centimetres (or -1 if no echo arrives). Then print a reading once per second.
Hint
The sequence is: drive TRIG LOW briefly, HIGH for 10 µs (delayMicroseconds(10)), back LOW. Then pulseIn(ECHO, HIGH, 30000) gives the width in µs; divide by 58.3. A return of 0 from pulseIn means the timeout expired — translate that into your -1 sentinel.
Show one solution
distance.ino C++ / Arduino
const uint8_t TRIG = 3;
const uint8_t ECHO = 2;

void setup() {
  Serial.begin(115200);
  pinMode(TRIG, OUTPUT);
  pinMode(ECHO, INPUT);
  digitalWrite(TRIG, LOW);
}

float distanceCm() {
  digitalWrite(TRIG, LOW);  delayMicroseconds(2);
  digitalWrite(TRIG, HIGH); delayMicroseconds(10);   // 10 us trigger
  digitalWrite(TRIG, LOW);

  unsigned long t = pulseIn(ECHO, HIGH, 30000UL);    // us, ~5 m cap
  if (t == 0) return -1.0f;                          // no echo
  return t / 58.3f;                                  // d_cm = t_us / 58.3
}

void loop() {
  float cm = distanceCm();
  if (cm < 0) Serial.println(F("no echo"));
  else { Serial.print(cm, 1); Serial.println(F(" cm")); }
  delay(1000);
}

The timeout is the production-grade detail: without it, a missing echo makes pulseIn block for up to a second and your control loop stalls. Capping at 30 ms bounds the worst case to ~5 m of range and keeps the loop responsive.

🎓 Chapter recap

You can now reason about a full signal chain from sensor to actuator: time an HC-SR04 echo and convert it to distance with $d_{\text{cm}} = t_{\mu s}/58.3$; decode a DHT one-wire frame and verify its checksum; protect every inductive load with a correctly oriented flyback diode ($v_L = L\,di/dt$ is why), and reach for an RC snubber on AC contacts; switch dangerous loads behind opto-isolated relays; drive a DC motor's direction with an H-bridge and its speed with PWM duty while avoiding shoot-through; and keep the whole thing alive with separate supplies, a common ground, decoupling, and an honest current budget. That's everything a small autonomous robot needs — sense, decide, act, and survive its own actuators.
